Keep sane on the plane: Tips for international flying

December 8, 2008 by Staff 

You’ve been planning this trip for weeks. It took nearly three hours, but your suitcase is finally packed with just the right balance of not-too-short shorts and neutral cardigans, an extra stick of deodorant and a copy of Rick Steves’ “Europe Through the Back Door.” You’ve contacted the bank to warn them of the excessive spending you’ll be doing at Prada, your hotel reservation has been confirmed three times, and you’ve been drinking plenty of water to gear up for the celebratory bottle of wine you’ll consume upon arrival. Yes, your European vacation of sunbathing and strolling is so close you can taste it.

But  first, you must take the flight to get there. Oh, that dreaded flight.

Despite its torturous reputation, international flying doesn’t have to be that bad. In fact, given the right attitude and a few simple tips, it can be a remarkably positive experience. Abide by these dos and don’ts and you’re guaranteed smooth sailing… well, flying.

DO pack light.

Even though you diligently searched your closet and spent hours packing the perfect suitcase, chances are you’ve packed too much. Before you haul that huge rolling duffel to the airport and pay an $80 overweight fine, sift back through your bag and remove anything you can’t see yourself wearing in the next 24 hours. This trick will get rid of any items you held up and thought, “Well, I might wear this.” You won’t.

The best policy for getting your belongings to your destination is to check one bag and carry on another. However, if your trip requires more than one connection, it might be best to fit everything in your carry-on to reduce the chance of lost luggage.  Jessi Tippett, a senior at the University of Georgia, flew to London over the summer for a study abroad program. She warns against the obvious mistake most make when packing only a carry-on: over packing. “I know the airlines occasionally lose people’s luggage, but my carry-on was so big and miserable that I would say it’s best to just pack as lightly as you can.” Allison Firor, an avid international traveler and film editor, agrees. “I ship essentials and large liquids ahead of me and try to pack as little as possible into a carry-on.”

If you decide to lug a carry-on and check a bag, be sure to pack the essentials in case your checked luggage gets lost. Sue Pyne, a travel agent from Cruise Planners, recommends at least one head-to-toe change of clothes that are easy to wash out, travel-size toiletries that comply with security regulations and comfortable walking shoes.

DON’T check in online.

Sometimes the most annoying (and avoidable) problems arise before you’ve even boarded the plane. Skip the hassle of mixed-up reservations, computer glitches and overall confusion by arriving at the airport early and checking in when you get there. Kathy Austin, a recreational traveler for more than 20 years, suggests arriving at least two hours ahead of time. Pyne agrees. “You have to check in at the airport anyway so the airline can be sure your passport is valid and it’s easiest to just drop your baggage right there.” Firor tends to arrive at the airport even earlier- three to four hours before her flight. “Checking in this early cuts the time you might spend standing in line at the check-in counter,” she says. “I’d rather kill an hour or two in an airline bookstore than stand in line at check-in.”

DO request a window seat.

Another perk of arriving early? First dibs on window seats. As most international fights are overnight, window seats are the prime (and most requested) location for resting your head and attempting to catch some shuteye. To avoid being crammed in a middle seat, request a seat location when you buy your ticket. Firor recommends a window seat just behind or on the wing and in or behind an exit row. “Never sit directly in front of the exit row, though,” she advises. “Those seats don’t recline all the way.” Be sure to request an aisle seat, however, if you get restless easily or need to move around a lot.

DON’T be afraid to eat.

Gone are the airplane mystery meats of yesteryear. Airlines have done a remarkable job improving the quality of in-flight food, which makes mealtime a surprisingly pleasant experience. If you feel a bit uneasy about the food, stick to basics like pastas or salads. “The simpler the food the better,” Austin says. Tippett, however, swears by the chicken. “Normally I wouldn’t have gotten it but it smelled really good.” Either way, airlines now offer a wide variety of meal choices and allow customers to make special requests for diabetic, vegetarian, kosher, or gluten-free cuisine when they book flights.

If you don’t want to risk eating airline food, snack packing is a must. Carry sliced fruit, peanuts, fruit bars, a small sandwich and lots of water to keep your energy up and prevent dehydration, Pyne suggests. Avoid soft drinks, coffee and sugary candies because they will only make you more thirsty and antsy.

DO be polite.

Your in-flight neighbors can be your best friend or worst enemy. Either way, you’re going to be spending the next 10 hours with them so it’s best to be friendly and clean to avoid unnecessary complications. If you are lucky enough to snag the coveted window seat, Firor suggests stowing all the items you won’t need on the flight in the overhead compartment so you won’t have to keep stepping over your neighbor.

“Maximize your space by keeping it organized,” Austin says. Hold on to a cup from dinner to place trash and wrappers in, keep your things under your seat and try to avoid getting in your seatmate’s personal space. If they fall asleep on your shoulder just as you need to use the lavatory, simply nudge them and excuse yourself. This will prevent the awkward moment that would follow if they woke up just as you were straddling their lap to get to the aisle.

DON’T take a sleeping pill.

As tempting as it sounds, self-medicating yourself into a near coma will only make your first day of vacation miserable. Skip the drowsiness and fatigue that come with sleeping pills and go au naturale. Sleeping might be difficult on a loud plane but it’s not impossible. If you’re sitting in an aisle seat, Tippett recommends reclining your seat all the way back and stretching your legs to the hand rest in front of you. If you’re sitting in a window seat, bring a pillow and prop your head up for maximum comfort.

Even if you find the most comfortable position, you might be too excited and jittery to sleep. Bring a magazine or Sudoku book to stay occupied or enjoy the music channels provided by the airline. Firor admits that her favorite part of the flight is the entertainment. “Watch every bit of entertainment there is on long flights,” she says. “I flip through everything before I get started.” Tippett strongly encourages bringing your own headphones if you plan to enjoy the on-flight entertainment. “The airplane headphones hurt really bad and are extremely uncomfortable.”

If the thought of spending the night on a plane sounds like pure horror, there are ways to get around it. Most international flights are overnight, but it is sometimes possible to find flights leaving in the morning and arriving in the evening. Pyne is an advocate of these flights. “If you can find a morning to evening flight, it might be better and easier on the body,” she says. “When you take those overnight flights, you’re up for almost 24 hours.” This can undoubtedly put a damper on your first day of vacation. Nevertheless, resist the temptation to nap when you arrive. This can throw your sleep pattern completely out of whack and leave you drowsy for days. Power through it and let the amazing sites you visit energize you and keep you on your feet.

Until we invent teleportation, international flights are a burden we all must bear to explore the corners of the world. But if you just relax and stick to these bits of advice, you just might have some fun before your real vacation even begins.
