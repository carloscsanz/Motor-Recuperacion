Out of whack

Edited by Andrew Hornery and Bonnie Malkin
January 19, 2005

Page Tools

  • Email to a friend
  • Printer format

Scarlet fever and a bad reaction to penicillin explained why the normally "ordered" life of the seasoned socialite Shari-Lea Hitchcock had turned to chaos and landed her in trouble with the law, Waverley Court was told yesterday. Hitchcock, Australia's most famous mistress, arrived at court in a chauffeur- driven silver limousine, which sped off with an older, unidentified man remaining in the car. She had already entered a guilty plea to various driving charges.

She appeared calm and collected as her lawyer, Chris Murphy, told the magistrate, Deborah Sweeney, that his client was "out of whack" when she drove her seven-year-old child (fathered by the cardboard king Richard Pratt) to school in October.

Murphy told the court that Hitchcock, who once worked in his law office, had no one else to drive her daughter to school. She drove while her licence was suspended (due to excessive demerit points), spoke on her mobile phone while driving and gave police false details when asked to identify herself.

Police said a patrol car had to take action to avoid a collision with a blue BMW sedan being driven by Hitchcock as it crossed double lines in Watsons Bay on October 10. To the allegation that she was using her mobile phone while driving, Hitchcock later told police: "I only flipped open my phone for a minute." Unable to produce her driver's licence, she told them: "I'm just taking my daughter to school" and identified herself as Elizabeth (Lizzie) Buttrose of Vaucluse, the niece of the journalist, author and arbiter of good manners Ita Buttrose.

Ms Sweeney said Hitchcock's ill health could have been a contributing factor to her behaviour, however it had been cancelled out by her "bad driving record", which featured 19 matters since 1989, including one for talking on her mobile while driving. She warned Hitchcock not to get behind the wheel again, disqualified her from driving for 12 months and fined her a total of $1000.

Outside the court Hitchcock, dressed in skin-tight black knee-length skirt, tight black top, black stiletto spikes and trademark fuchsia pink toe nails, said she thought the outcome was fair.

Dress press

Lady (Sonia) McMahon's revealing presence on the red carpet at the Golden Globes (Spike yesterday) caused a few ripples in the US press, as well as in the Australian papers. But it was not quite in the same league as her 1971 front page headlines.

"Aww," remarked TV Guide in the US. "Nip/Tuck scoundrel Julian McMahon strolled the carpet with his mother, Lady Sonia McMahon." The report added, presumably for the few Americans who don't have a strong command of Australian history: "Don't forget McMahon's a blue blood from Down Under whose father was Australia's prime minister."

One for the boys

Sydneysiders have responded with gusto to Spike's request for weird and wonderful water-saving tips.

A clear favourite among those who emailed in was the time-honoured maxim: "If it's yellow let it mellow; if it's brown flush it down, " which Margaret Combley informs us has been in use since the water shortages of World War II.

But that was just the tip of the iceberg. Paul Hunt, of Engadine, has some tips for the male of the species. "Men only, living in homes with a backyard: pop out the back, turn the lights off and do your last number on the grass. This will save hundreds of thousands of flushes in Sydney alone. Make sure no one is looking as you could get charged with indecent exposure, and change location periodically or the grass will die."

In a lather

Quirky practices aside, Spike also received some serious suggestions. Showering seemed to attract the most attention.

"Since moving into a new unit where we have about a half dozen plants on the balcony, to save water I fill a bucket with the cold water from the shower while waiting for the hot water to run through," said Sophie Powell. "I then transfer this to a watering can which I use to water the plants. No water wasted."

Another reader suggested turning off the shower when soaping up. "Growing up in Sydney and being one of eight children, every evening my father's mantra at shower time would follow along the lines of: Hop in, get wet. Tap off, hop out. Soap up, hop in. Rinse off, tap off."

Talk it up

Like a couple of lovesick teenagers, the talk show hosts from opposite sides of the pond Andrew Denton and Michael Parkinson giggled and looked into one another's eyes during a cosy lunch at Otto's spaghetti joint in Woolloomooloo yesterday. We're assured the old chums were just catching up, although it was only last July Denton hosed down speculation he would step into the slot vacated by Parkinson at the BBC. Denton even flew to London and reportedly chatted with BBC executives, but later told the local press he would be staying put.
