Well, yeah, MAC owned that industry for years, but....

Cost/performance still doesn't favor the MAC. It's excellent hardware, no doubt, and given your specific intent, a good choice if price isn't such an issue.

However, while Photoshop is threaded, it's limited to the extent that it doesn't achieve double the performance. SMP will give you much smoother response if you're opening multiple applications, and I THINK it helps if you have multiple images.

On the PC side, the high memory bandwidth of PC800 RAM (or the newer stuff, whatever they call that 533Mhz version) coupled with the higher speed AGP performance of most of the Intel based boards tends to favor the recent P4 CPU's for Photoshop and similar applications.

I'm an AMD fan, myself, but then I'm also more interested in 3D rendering performance than pure Photoshop performance, so I'd have to say that a high end AMD would be a price conscious solution, but a fair one.

Here's an interesting review. While it's not exactly Photoshop, the performance bottlenecks are similar for Adobe After Effects, as reviewed here:

http://www.digitalvideoediting.com/cgi-bin/getframeletter.cgi?%2F2002%2F05_may%2Ffeatures%2Fc w_aeshowdown.htm

They compare a MAC with dual processors against a typical dual MP1800. The dual MP1800 is usually about 30% ahead of the MAC on all benchmarks.

In addition to that, they use a different color standard than PCs which is superior in the context of graphic arts.

Pardon my questioning this point, but I'm not sure I buy this claim. I acknowledge that the first Pantone matched monitors were probably made for the MAC, but color is generated by math using the primary light sources of red, green and blue. I don't think any MAC has more than 32bit color, like most current PC cards, and many monitors are interchangeable between the platforms.

Color, with respect to Photoshop, is a function of the software. Depending on the color model you choose, you might be referencing ink pigments in cyan, magenta and yellow, or one of the many other color models available. These models can be accurately converted from one to the other with simple math, only a little more complicated than a conversion from miles to kilometers.

I think what you noticed was the color selector options in Photoshop on the MAC (or some other software), which are identicle in the PC, but may have been set to one of the other standards when you used it. Depending on the output target, various models can be a superior choice, but it's not MAC specific, to my knowledge.

I'd REALLY like to know if I'm wrong about this, as I'm and engineer and I've written plugins for 3DSMax, Maya and others.
