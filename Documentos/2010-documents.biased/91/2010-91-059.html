After about four days in Plane Maker to model it twice after the first iteration showed significant discrepancies with published dimensions, here's the first flight of a still cockpit-less Learjet 23. I'm planning on a dimensional tolerance of < 2% where published dimensions are available. The in-cockpit viewpoint will probably depart from reality in the interests of getting an acceptable view, as the seating position on most aircraft is too near the panel. That will take a number of iterations to achieve.

Flight parameters are another matter as there's lots of data from accident reports, etc., and I'll have to pick what is crucial. I'm getting the sense that this will not be an XP walk-on-and-fly aircraft, so to speak. Compared to the Mozkeet, this will require stricter adherence to systems and flight limits to avoid becoming a smear on the landscape. This first test-flight showed the aircraft to be quite unstable in roll, so much so that I had to check if I screwed up the symmetry somewhere. I've assumed a compressor diameter of 15" but it's proving hard to slow in flight (haven't set Cd's yet or a host of other parameters).

This will take a long time to complete so I'm marking the first day of the project as February 14, 2010 for (my) future reference. As soon as I complete the external model in PM I'll be going back to the Mozkeet to finish some fixes for this month. The exported PM object will be the basis for the 3D model done in ac3d using the process outlined in this post showing the obsolete model.

 lj23_pm_flite02.jpg (62.3K)
Number of downloads: 68


It is a bit early to ask for help but if anyone comes across obscure hard data in the web or has any requests as to what to emphasize in this project please post a link/comment here. I could use help in getting a working radar done (that's why I've skipped the CF-104). Fuel management will be one area of emphasis. The engines draw fuel only from the wing tanks. Fuel pumps transfer fuel from the tip tanks to the wing tanks so the former empty first. I can do that with a plugin but I'm not sure if Plane Maker can do that by itself. Any ideas on that? The fuselage tank (if included) transfers only to both wing tanks. While that improves the CoG, the first priority is to use up the fuel in the tip tanks. Fuel can dribble back to the tip tanks so the latter's pumps must be cycled from time to time (an orange light indicates when those pumps can be turned off). Imbalances are consequential even on the ground.

I might add later variants too all the way to the C-21A, a personal favorite.

Boxes ticked for the LJ20-30 series configuration are numbered from left tip tank to right with no.5 being a fuse tank, if available. To vent from, say, tank2 to tank1 for dumping purposes you'd tick tank2 feeds tank1 and transfer in that direction when tank1 is venting. I don't think the LJ23 could vent from anything but the tip tanks so that's all you'd vent in an emergency. The first model couldn't dump fuel. Besides an overall maximum landing weight there's a limit for how much fuel in the tip tanks one is allowed to land with.

Gravity feed would be assumed if one tank connects to another and the fuel pump box is not ticked. Engine fuel pump is assumed to suffice if a tank feeds an engine and the tank's fuel pump box is not ticked. Equal distribution is assumed when one tank feeds two or more tanks. Bidirectional fuel transfer capability is assumed when two tanks feed each other.

I read somewhere that LJ23 drivers were obsessed with fuel management; I understand why. Fuel fill is at the tip tanks, and they are filled alternatively subject to an imbalance limit. I believe an imbalance of more than 800-lbs. could tip the plane laterally in a ground turn. In flight such an imbalance could not be trimmed-out under max control deflection. Fuel from the tips trickle to the wing tanks and from there probably to the fuselage tank (subject to switch configuration) and could also lead to the plane squatting on its tail under certain load configurations.

It's easy to error check the above with the cockpit switches required if they are not included in the panel. By default a fuel pump is assumed to be always on if engines are running and no fuel pump switch is included in the panel.

Gravity trickle and unusable fuel per tank should probably remain in plugin territory, as should consequences. For the LJ23, a leak in the fuselage fuel tank could presumably trigger a fire; a leak from a tip or wing tank would probably just vent outside.

After examining the datarefs I was disappointed to see that for X-Plane's 9 fuel tanks the only transfer options are left-right-center. That is, XP's fuel transfer simulation lags XP's fuel tank simulation. To make matters worse, non-writeable datarefs could get in the way. I doubt if Vs 9.5 has anything new in this area. One way of kludging this is by getting the plugin to do rapid fuel transfer cycling:

On EVEN flight cycles the LEFT tip tank transfers to the LEFT wing tank; on ODD flight cycles, the RIGHT tip tank transfers to the RIGHT wing tank. A similar process trickles fuel from the wings to their tip tanks if the pumps are off and the plane is pulling Gs in a bank and I need to use the fuel transfer algorithm. For the fuselage tank, if available, and with the appropriate switch configuration set, it will transfer to the LEFT wing tank on EVEN flight cycles and to the RIGHT wing tank on ODD flight cycles.

This means a lot of writing to- and from- transfer datarefs — a workaround until we get proper fuel-transfer simulation from Austin. Care must be taken with annunciator lights so they don't flicker. All this must be unwound when we do get proper fuel-transfer algorithms - tripling the whole effort. Nevertheless I'm so pleased with this idea that I'm giving it an acronym:


RFTC™ = Rapid Fuel Transfer Cycling  


You read it here first

Dunno if it'll work but I wish there was a better way.

Edit: That means the transfer rate will be halved as both transfer pumps can't be on at the same time. On the other hand, it probably works too fast anyway so halving it will only make it more realistic. (My philosophy is to use PM/XP resources as much as possible.)
