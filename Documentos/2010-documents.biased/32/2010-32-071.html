  • SIGIR 2009 conference notes

    27Jul
    Categories: Information Retrieval, Information Science

    The 32th ACM SIGIR conference, July 18-23, 2009. Boston, MA, U.S.A.

    I enjoyed the SIGIR conference and would like to share some of my notes.

    Weimao at JFK Library, SIGIR09 in Boston

    1ST KEYNOTE

    Susan Dumais, the Gerard Salton Award recipient, gave the first keynote speech titled An Interdisciplinary Perspective on Information Retrieval, in which she reviewed her personal research journey and touched on the continuous growth of the Web and advances of search systems. Looking to the future, Dumais asserted that, in 10 years, the “rectangle” world of search systems (e.g., in the form of search boxes) will change. She discussed future challenges in the face of dynamic information, temporal contexts of queries, and longevity of terms.

    2ND KEYNOTE ON POWER-LAW AND SCALE-FREE

    The second keynote was given by Albert-Laszlo Barabasi, who recently moved from Notre Dame to Northeastern U. From a complex network perspective, Barabasi offered a classic review of common characteristics of a wide range of systems from the WWW to genetic networks. Particularly, he presented the scale-free model (one of his major contributions in the field), in which both network growth and preferential attachment are essentially needed to explain the common presence of power-law distributions.

    Through visualization demos, Barabasi showed interesting patterns about system robustness in random and scale-free models. The research has implications on how IR systems can function in large-scale, distributed/networked settings. Barabasi discussed a “fitness” function to support “late-comers” in network growth but left out other critical issues such as limited local capacities and aging effect. As Dave Lewis commented during Q&A, some degree distribution plots may look power-law (roughly linear on log/log) but are not truthfully so — additional factors were missing in the models being discussed. Overall, the talk was very interesting and relevant to my personal interest in distributed/decentralized IR — the content was not new though.

    POWER-LAWS IN IR

    Barabasi’s power-law view of complex systems connects well with IR research. Donald Metzler from Yahoo! labs presented a work motivated by the power-law distribution of anchor text, in which the majority of web pages lack external anchor texts (through in-links) that can be used to enrich document representation. Relying on internal links within a domain, a diffusion mechanism was developed to overcome anchor text sparsity. A related research, presented by Zhicheng Zhou from Microsoft Research, challenged the link independence assumption and analyzed source page relationships to adjust anchor text weights. Jimmy Lin from U Maryland discussed the implication of Zipf law (a special type of power-law) word frequency distribution on parallel IR operations and load balancing in MapReduce environments.

    RELEVANCE: RISKY BUSINESS

    Jun Wang and Jianhan Zhu from University College London presented two papers that borrowed ideas from the Portfolio Theory to guide relevance ranking in IR. They reasoned that retrieval ranking should take into account not only document relevance (estimates) but also uncertainty (risk) about relevance estimates. Sometimes, highly relevant items are highly risky, that is, relevance estimates are associated with large variances. So depending on the extent to which a system is willing to take the risk of ranking, different strategies can be used to rank documents. For example, one may rank documents to maximize (discounted) mean relevance scores or to minimize variance of relevance estimates. Interestingly, it was further demonstrated that relevance variance is associated with the correlation between two documents — with the uncertainty/risk/variance factor taken into account, the model is capable of diversifying documents in the rank list.

    LESS IS MORE

    The phenomenon of “less is more” appeared in several presentations/posters. A. Oulasvirta from Helsinki U of Technology presented Less is More: Paradox of Choice for Search Engine Use, in which a fake search engine interface showing 6 relevant documents significantly outperformed another interface presenting 24 items — users seemed to be more satisfied and confident with fewer items in result lists. Xavier Amatriain from Telefonica Research presented a collaborative filtering (recommender) system based on expert opinions, or the so-called “wisdom of the few.” It was shown that using ratings from a small set of experts achieved comparable results and users seemed to prefer recommendations generated from expert opinions. Neal Lathia from University College London presented a poster on temporal collaborative filtering and showed that adding more data (over time) to a recommender model does not necessarily improve prediction accuracy.

    LARGE SCALE DISTRIBUTED SYSTEMS

    The 7th Workshop on Large-Scale Distributed System for Information Retrieval hosted two keynotes and six papers dealing with large scale IR infrastructures and P2P systems. The first keynote by Leonidas Kontothanassis from Google discussed storage and delivery machanisms used to support traffic intensive operations for video viewing and downloading on Youtube. The second keynote presented DryadLINQ, a Microsoft approach to high performance IR operations through parallel computing on a PC cluster. Participants raised several questions about its similarities with the widely adopted MapReduce paradigm. In particular, two papers examined important challenges in MapReduce, e.g., Jimmy Lin’s short paper on the implication of Zipf law distribution on load balancing. A couple of papers focused on building distributed indices in structured P2P environments for popular item search. Javed and I had a paper investigating the clustering paradox in distributed IR environments, in which neither weak clustering nor strong clustering of distributed collections are desirable for decentralized search. The workshop, with roughly 20 participants, was less formal and quite interactive.
