Conception, Evolution, and Application of Functional Programming Languages

While I was doing some research for myself about the evolution of the different families of functional programming languages, I came across an interesting ACM Computing Survey by Paul Hudak, published in 1989, about the conception, evolution, and application of functional programming languages.

The foundations of functional programming languages are examined from both historical and technical perspectives. Their evolution is traced through several critical periods: early work on lambda calculus and combinatory calculus, Lisp, Iswim, FP, ML, and modern functional languages such as Miranda’ and Haskell. The fundamental premises on which the functional programming methodology stands are critically analyzed with respect to philosophical, theoretical, and pragmatic concerns. Particular attention is paid to the main features that characterize modern functional languages: higher-order functions, lazy evaluation, equations and pattern matching, strong static typing and type inference, and data abstraction. In addition, current research areas-such as parallelism, nondeterminism, input/output, and state-oriented computations-are examined with the goal of predicting the future development and application of functional languages.
As in recent years functional programming languages have gained more and more ground in real-world applications, I thought this paper might interest you. And if you haven't had the pleasure of learning functional programming yet, it might also help you finding out which family of functional languages interests you the most. Besides that, it is also interesting to see how the mentioned research areas have produced incredible results over the past years.

On Source Code Annotations

"The best comments aid the understanding of a program by briefly pointing out salient details or by providing a larger-scale view of the proceedings."

            - Brian Kernighan and Rob Pike (in The Practice of Programming)



Task-related source code annotations (eg. "TODO:", "XXX:", "FIXME:", "OPTIMIZE:", etc.), even though common practice, are a bit like dry, heavy and full-bodied french wines. You either love or hate them (at least many people do). And the matter of how (contemporary) useful these comments are seems to be just another topic, which will probably start uncountably more discussions for another couple of decades.

This is actually something I planned on writing about for a while now. To me it is just interesting how heated some people's opinions about these annotations are. Some (in this case Scot Mcphee) even go so far to say that a build should fail if the source code includes this kind of comments. It is obvious that if a project makes use of a bug tracker it should be preferred to issue a ticket, as it would be a formal software engineering practice. Still, in many projects, including some of mine, source code annotations are a common practice.

At the 30th International Conference on Software Engineering in 2008, Margaret-Anne Storey, Jody Ryall, R. Ian Bull, Del Myers, and Janice Singer presented a paper called TODO or to bug: exploring how task annotations play a role in the work practices of software developers. I don't know how widely this work is known within the larger programming community, but it gives some interesting insights. The questions asked during a survey that was conducted as part of the study are as follows:

  1. Which annotations do developers create to support their programming tasks?
  2. Why do developers create these annotations?
  3. How do these annotations support developers’ informal and formal work practices?
  4. Are task annotations kept up-to-date or are they forgotten as the code evolves?
  5. What processes do the developers use for managing annotations?

The whole study was largely based on developers active within the Eclipse community. Still, 54% of the survey submissions told that they never use Eclipse's bookmarks and another 30% answered that they only use them rarely. Several additionally interviewed developers are said to be using TODO annotations within their source code, though with a tendency towards smaller tasks. Besides a varied management of the TODOs within the projects of the interviewed developers, one of the sections that should interest us the most is Cost/Benefit Analysis and TODO creation which shows us the balance between annotations and tickets:

Developers discussed the costs and benefits of creating a bug over a “quick TODO”. For tasks that could be fulfilled quickly, the developers stated that there was little point in opening a bug or work item report. There were also concerns with the overhead associated with formalizing a task (emails sent to colleagues being one of them). One developer said that his TODOs were for very small tasks: “But I mean small… I don’t mean a five line change. A five line change might have huge implications in the behavior”. UserInterface Dev1 mentioned that because his project was widely used in other projects, he sometimes left a TODO alone because “if there's a problem and somebody comes across it, they have no qualms about opening a bug”.
Anyhow, most IDEs and other development environments and text-editors provide (more or less complete) capabilities to manage these annotations, like Visual Studio, Eclipse, and rake notes for Rails. These tools are assisting in actually getting back to the annotations and organizing them in general. Even with the current helpers, one of the most common contrary arguments against code annotations remains that once these are made they are rarely gotten back to and easily forgotten, as section 5.4.2 - Maintenance Issues and Out-of-date TODOs shows.

A recurring theme throughout all the interviews was a concern with out-of-date annotations. The developers mentioned that these can clutter the code and obscure more meaningful information, or can even negatively affect code comprehension. Code cleanliness was an important consideration for some of the developers we interviewed, as they believe that “clean” code is easier to understand and maintain. When task annotations are stored directly in the code, they become an artifact that needs to be maintained. This is in contrast to comments stored in CVS or Bugzilla, where the context is stored alongside the annotation.

Several reasons for out-of-date annotations were described in the interviews. Sometimes the task was done, but the TODO was not removed. In other cases, they were left for future reference. Sometimes the tasks are simply not attended to. As Backend Dev1 reported: “we all put these comments in our code and then don’t look back at them. None of them actually remind us...”. A further complication arises when a programmer leaves a project and also leaves behind TODOs that others do not understand but cannot easily remove. Some developers seem either reluctant to or ambivalent about changing or removing someone else’s task annotations. As UserInterface Dev1 reported: “The ones that stay forever are the ones that are unnamed, like the ones that are ’TODO this‘, and just the line. And you look at it, and you don't know who it came from or what really it's about or what it was accomplished for. Or what the TODO was relevant for.”
Obviously, more contrary and loosely related research has been conducted as well, as M. J. Kaelbling's paper Programming languages should NOT have comment statements (ACM SIGPLAN Notices Volume 23, Issue 10) shows, although this work discusses source code comments in general, and should not be of further interest in this particular matter.

We should have a pretty good and authentic overview of how developers in general think about annotations. It doesn't matter if it derives from the papers mentioned in this article of from our own experience. The before mentioned paper includes a chart of the life expectancy of the annotations within the projects the interviewed developers are participating in. Only 30% to less than 10% of the annotations are remaining for more than 3 months and after about one year and a 4 months both projects show a remainder of close to 0. This already shows us that developers seem to be disciplined. While in my opinion we probably won't ever (even need to) get rid of this type of comments, we could still find some ways to utilize them in a more efficient manner.

A lot of recent research effort went into exactly that. One example is Lin Tan, Ding Yuan, and Yuanyuan Zhou's work HotComments: How to Make Program Comments More Useful?. This paper explores comments beyond their current usage, and specifically studies the feasibility and benefits of automatically analyzing comments to detect software bugs and bad comments. The results are promising. The presented algorithm detected 12 bugs in the Linux sources, with 2 confirmed by kernel developers, by using 137 rules extracted from the comments. This is a whole new approach to bug detection as previous tools only extracted rules and invariants from the source code itself or execution traces, and not the comments. Another approach, although around for a while longer, are annotation languages, such as SAL annotations and Java annotations. Apple's XCode takes another way and uses #pragma mark in Objective-C to "organize" annotations.

After all, it seems like many programmers often see either black or white, not the grey and possibly bright colors in between. Instead of cursing and judging, we should may be just improve our current practices? I will leave the rest up to further discussion.

Web vs. Desktop Applications: The Neverending Debate

We can read about it every single day. One person describes the advantages of web applications over their native counterparts. Another one dismisses every single argument of the previous critique. This week, the web vs. desktop application debate gained new momentum after Jeff Atwood commented on Michael Braude's post Why I’ll Never Be A ‘Web Guy’. Michael initially wrote:

The reason most people want to program for the web is that they're not smart enough to do anything else. They don't understand compilers, concurrency, 3D or class inheritance. They haven't got a clue why I'd use an interface or an abstract class. They don't understand: virtual methods, pointers, references, garbage collection, finalizers, pass-by-reference vs. pass-by-value, virtual C++ destructors, or the differences between C# structs and classes. They also know nothing about process. Waterfall? Spiral? Agile? Forget it. They've never seen a requirements document, they've never written a design document, they've never drawn a UML diagram, and they haven't even heard of a sequence diagram.
But they do know a few things: they know how to throw an ASP.NET webpage together, send some (poorly done) SQL down into a database, fill a dataset, and render a grid control. This much they've figured out. And the chances are good it didn't take them long to figure it out.

So forgive me for being smarmy and offensive, but I have no interest in being a 'web guy'. And there are two reasons for this. First, it's not a challenging medium for me. And second, because the vast majority of Internet companies are filled with bad engineers - precisely because you don't need to know complicated things to be a web developer. As far as I'm concerned, the Internet is responsible for a collective dumbing down of our intelligence. You just don't have to be that smart to throw up a webpage.

I really hope everybody's wrong and everything doesn't "move to the web." Because if it does, one day I will either have to reluctantly join this boring movement, or I'll have to find another profession.
Of course, as in all of his posts, Jeff takes a heavy position on the opposite, saying that for an increasingly large percentage of users, the desktop application is already dead and all programming will be web programming. The first already is and the latter eventually may be true.

Due to the nature of online debates, and the popularity of Jeff's blog, it unsurprisingly spawned off follow-up posts. This time, Andy Matuschak is his opponent, saying that the desktop already has tons of advantages; why not spend our time removing its disadvantages instead? He's got a point there. But still, it is inevitable that the web will eventually win, even in the niche markets that Jeff predicts to stay dominated by desktop applications. Cheap, and sheer endless storage on demand, almost instant availability on demand, all of that is just too temping. For everyone. It is still a long path, though.

One of the most interesting areas will be how web applications are going to replace our current software development environments. Mozilla Bespin is a first step towards this future. But, for example, how are we going to create, test and deploy in a robust, and user-friendly manner from a development environment in the cloud? Also in the future, the social aspect in software engineering will play a more and more important role. Sharing ideas, knowledge and artifacts among groups and their members plays an important part here, and many online solutions have already been developed. GitHub and Lighthouse are two of the more prominent examples. An interesting survey on this topic is provided by Navid Ahmadi, Mehdi Jazayeri, Francesco Lelli, and Sasa Nesic in their work A Survey of Social Software Engineering, presented at the First International Workshop on Social Software Engineering and Applications. Many of the solutions we have and dream of wouldn't and possible without the web.

The tools we are currently developing web applications with are playing another important and heated part in general discussions and debates. Is JavaScript, at all sufficient as a foundation for future web applications, no matter how useful it, its frameworks, and Ajax currently are? There certainly is a demand for alternatives, as a client side implementation of the Python programming language called Skulpt, working without any requirement for browser plugins, shows. Current applications developed with help of Javascript and Ajax get surprisingly close to the standard of desktop applications. Google Docs shows that. However, underlying technologies used for presentation, transmission and encoding are still the same as in regular Web 1.0 applications, which is well known, almost obvious, and also supported by Michael Cooper's 2007 paper Accessibility of Emerging Rich Web Technologies: Web 2.0 and the Semantic Web. This article also explains the difficulties and inabilities our current technologies have with accessibility. Large software companies are spending tremendous amounts of human resources, time and money in designing accessibility features for their products. Walter Kern showed in his paper Web 2.0 - End of Accessibility? that this is mainly caused by the use of Javascript and Ajax. Both have important roles in dynamism and responsiveness in today's web applications.

On demand applications without the need for installation, if web based or not, are definitely and inevitably coming. In some regards they are already here. To conclude this post, one last thing pops into my mind. In Peter Seibel's interview with Brad Fitzpatrick, Google employee and creator of LiveJournal, which is about to be published in his upcoming book Coders at Work, Brad said about desktop software:

The problem is, no one uses it. You want to write stuff people use, which comes down to web apps.

Sneak Preview: Many Famous Coders at Work

We all know: There are two types of books about programming. The first delivering direct knowledge about a specific topic or a specific programming language or technology. Two of my personal favorite, and more accessible books of this category are C Programming Language by Kernighan and Ritchie and Real World Haskell by Bryan O'Sullivan. Books of the other category talk about programming in general. I like to call this kind of books programming meta books. They are widely applicable and of interest to the field in general, even while they might contain source code written in a particular language. These books have the habit of being timeless and easy to digest. Just between us, I always carry a book of this category with me, read them in the tube on my way to, and from work, in the evening before going to sleep and in the mornings while eating my daily bowl of muesli. Famous examples of this category are Structure and Interpretation of Computer Programs by Abelson and Sussman, Programming Pearls by Jon Bentley, Code Complete by Steve McConnell and Beautiful Code by Andy Oram and Greg Wilson.

The newest addition to the latter list of books might turn out to be Peter Seibel's new book Coders at Work, due to be placed into a book shelf near you next month. He didn't spare any expenses and visited 15 of the most famous and influential programmers of our time, including Simon Peyton Jones, Joe Armstrong, Peter Norvig, Ken Thompson and Donald Knuth. Peter was so kind to give me access to his new book and I couldn't wait to start reading it. And in one sentence: It is absolutely amazing! A page turner, just like Harry Potter for the technically minded.

The interviews start out with asking how the interviewee got into programming. This seemingly simple questions already yields fascinating answers. Due to the varying age it gives you a very personal view on how computer science education has changed in the past century: Joe Armstrong wrote his first programs in FORTRAN on an IBM mainframe at his local council, Donald Knuth had his first programming experience as a freshman at Case Institute of Technology, Simon Peyton-Jones wrote his first procedures on an obscure 20 touch-key keyboard and a television connected to an IBM schools computer, up to Brad Fitzpatrick, who learned to program on an Apple II made from old spare parts.

But the most amazing part of this book is probably the honesty the sometimes very opinionated interviews radiate. Most interviews these days have a very reserved tone, but not the ones in this book. It feels like, for example, Joe Armstrong is sitting right next to you. Anyways, without wanting to offend anyone, the interview we all are probably looking the most forward to is the one with Donald Knuth.

It actually had a more humble tone than I expected, which says a lot about Knuth's personality, which reflects in many of his statements during the interview, like in the practicability of Art of Computer Programming:

The things that I leave out are where somebody has a data structure that saves a factor of log log n only when n gets bigger than two to the million. And there are lots and lots of papers that are doing that. They’re playing games where in principal, if computers were godlike, then we could have algorithms that are faster. But even an algorithm like a balanced tree or AVL tree, I don’t use in my own programs unless I know that it’s going to be a really big tree.
Oh, and in case you wonder what he is using insted:

I use an ordinary binary search tree with a little trick for randomizing it that I just put in.
After all, have you ever wondered about what the creator of Erlang thinks about object-oriented programming? Why JavaScript's global object is the window object? What Daniel Henry Holmes Ingalls, Jr., who created Smalltalk together with Alan Kay, wishes to be adopted into the programming language mainstream? Then I can't recommend this book enough. It is worth every single penny. Pre-orders can currently be placed over at Amazon, and probably at your local book shop.

Also, if you would like to know more about Peter Seibel himself, don't miss out on today's issue of The other side of the table.

Evolution of the Google File System

This week seems to be review week. While I would love to finish some more technical posts, a new article in ACM Queue stopped me from actually doing that. The article I am talking about is called GFS: Evolution on Fast-forward. Herein, Marshall Kirk McKusick, an expert on file systems and known for the original design of the Berkeley FFS (Fast File System) and intensive work on UFS, interviews Sean Quinlan, who served as the GFS tech leader for a couple of years, about the history and evolution of the Google File System.

As Ralph Johnson already mentioned earlier on his blog, right away the first question concerning that the original GFS architecture is based on a single master yields probably one of the most interesting answers. Rather than building the file system upon a distributed master, the engineers have chosen to rather "get something out into the hands of users much more rapidly than would have otherwise been possible". As soon as they started to run into problems, Google's engineers decided to move away from a single-master architecture and emphasized on tuning the master performance. Another interesting aspect of the initial design process is that the team working on GFS was not larger than three members, but still managed to get the system ready for deployment in less than a year.

The article continues with a discussion of a file-count problems GFS initially experienced. Another topic is initial emphasis of the system on on batch efﬁciency as opposed to low latency. Consistency is covered as well, but you should really read for yourself, if you are interested in file systems, scaling on unimaginable magnitudes or just the guts of Google's architecture.

How Does Pair Programming Work?

November last year, Stuart Wray, senior lecturer at the Royal School of Signals in Blandford, United Kingdom, submitted a paper called How does pair programming work? to IEEE Software, which today showed up as a pre-print on the IEEE's website.

Anecdotes and experiments show that pair programming sometimes brings benefits and sometimes doesn’t. Recent studies have cast doubt on the “driver-navigator” metaphor, often used to explain pair programming. We need a new theory to explain why pair programming is sometimes useful and sometimes not. This article proposes four distinct mechanisms, by which pair programming may improve the performance of developers, and explains the psychological basis of each mechanism. With this theoretical explanation in hand, we may be better placed to predict when we should use pair programming.
The paper starts off explaining and defining the basic principles of pair programming such as the driver and the navigator. After that Stuart talks about how he discovered pair programming while studying at Cambridge around 1980 without being aware of it, and that a few years later he realized that this practice was already widely known within the professional software development community. Following this little tour, the paper discusses the psychological and cognitive processes involved in pair programming. Conversation between the programmers is an emphasis of the discussion, as are the human elements in general. Finally, the conclusions are that the traditional “driver-navigator” metaphor indeed fails to describe the experiences and happenings in successful pair programming teams. Overall 4 mechanisms common in pair programming have been presented and the fourth - pair programmers may judge expertise better and transfer expertise better - has been identified as the most important one.

Another very interesting paper about pair programming, which still seems to be a hot topic in software engineering research. On the first look it might not seem like something most of us couldn't somehow feel while pair programming, however, the slightly different point of view was definitely refreshing.

Congratulations to Susan Dumais

Today, the ACM issued a press release that Susan Dumais from Microsoft Research (formerly Bell Labs/Bellcore) received the Gerard Salton Award for her innovative contributions to information indexing and retrieval systems that have widely impacted the quality of search from the desktop to the Web.

Susan Dumais joined Microsoft Research in 2007 where she is a Principal Researcher in the Adaptive Systems and Interaction Group, and works on algorithms and interfaces for improved information retrieval and general topic in human-computer interaction. She is a fellow of the ACM since 2006, was elected to the CHI Academy in 2005 and has published more than 170 articles in her career. From 1999-2003 she was chair of the ACM's Special Interest Group on Information Retrieval, SIGIR. She is also co-author/associate editor of the book Handbook of Applied Cognition, published together with Francis T. Durso (editor), Raymond S. Nickerson, Stephan Lewandowsky and Timothy J. Perfect at John Wiley and Sons.

The Gerard Salton Award is presented every three years to a researcher who has made "... significant, sustained and continuing contributions to research in information retrieval". This is the first time the award has been given to a member of a corporate research institute. Her Salton award lecture at SIGIR 2009 last month, "An Interdisciplinary Perspective on Information Retrieval", talked about her history and background in information retrieval research, growth of the Web and advances of search systems, and how the happenings, problems and social leaps within the past 15 years have made it an enormously interesting area. Next to various discussions of future challenges in information retrievel, the most interesting assertion in her speech was most likely the prediction of a shift away from the traditional, "rectangular" search box. I'm curious to know how that will turn out.

Congratulations!

Software Development and Philosophy

Yesterday, Andy Matuschak posted an article called Manifesto on his blog Square Signals. In this post he asks himself the question "What do you want to do when you grow up?".

I remember being asked that question at least a hundred times when I was a child. No matter if the people asking were teachers, family members, friends, whoever, the question was the same. When I was six years old I got my first computer, which my father bought second hand from our local university. The data center admins must have been a bunch of very nice people as they didn't only reformat the hard disks, they also installed a fresh copy of 386BSD. They also included a round about 200 page print out on how to use the OS and placed a sticky note containing the root login onto the front page. My mother was so kind to translate the English manual to German for me and I started to play around and kind of got hooked immediately. When ever I was asked again what I wanted to do when I grow up I responded: "I want to work with computers".

If someone would ask me today, my answer would be different. I would probably answer that I want to make other people's life's easier. That is what most of us actually do. Some of us want to change the way people work together, how they communicate with each other, how they find information they need, how they get entertained, and so on. Long things short: We want to change the world, or as Eric Clapton would say in his song Change the World: "If I could reach the stars I'd pull one down for you".

Changing the world is an important topic in philosophy. Karl Marx wrote in the eleventh and last Theses on Feuerbach that "the philosophers have only interpreted the world in various ways; the point is to change it". We are in a lucky position. Many human beings and companies all over the world are putting tremendous effort into philanthropy, which of course is a good thing. We just do our job, which in a vast amount of the cases has a direct impact on how people experience their life. It might make their work easier, helps them complete often tedious tasks within seconds, it can even higher their life expectancy, for example, by helping health professionals with computer-aided diagnosis. And according to Terrell Ward Bynum's and James H. Moor's book The Digital Phoenix: How Computers are Changing Philosophy it also works the other way round: Computers are having a significant impact upon foundational concepts in philosophy, such as the mind, consciousness, reasoning, logic, knowledge, truth and creativity. Many of us are already aware of this, others are not. One of many examples is Google's project 10^100, which offers ten million dollars for ideas that can change the world in categories like community, energy, environment, health, education and shelter.

Computers and philosophy walk hand in hand, completing each other. Both, practitioners (the most of us) and the theoreticians have the ultimate goal of changing the world, whether they are aware of it or not. This is what it comes down to, no matter what profession or personal goals we have.
