Indexing As A Craft

An indexer was a person who worked with an author and publisher to create a listing of topics called an index. The most common example is the back-of-the-book index.
"Indexing, far from being merely the alphabetically arranged concatenation of text words, is, first of all, a creative process. Though always, by definition, dependent on the work of another creative mind--that of a writer, artist, or compiler of data--it transforms the original text into a homologous but functionally different structure. In that structure, topics and names mentioned here and there in the text are arranged in an ordered and easily scannable sequence, indicating their place in the text. At the same time, both their explicit and implicit relationships are displayed in a way in which the original text does not or cannot reveal them."

"Like other creative processes, such as literary writing, painting, or composing music, indexing relies on certain technical rules which can be learned by example, training, and experience. Yet the creative inspiration that will result in a good and useful index can neither be learned nor taught."

Hans H. Wellisch Indexing from A to Z, 1995

Gerard Salton and Automatic Indexing

Prior to Gerry Salton, information retrieval was done with paper methods and library practices. Salton developed information retrieval for digital documents in a database context. A key innovation was automatic indexing - using machine methods to determine the meaning of text.
He was awarded the first ACM SIGIR Award for outstanding contributions to information retrieval, twice received the ASIS award for Best Information Science Book, and the ASIS Award of Merit, The Alexander von Humboldt Senior Scientist Award, and was recognized as a fellow of the American Association for the Advancement of Science.

A Simple Automatic Indexing Process

In 1983, Salton and McGill published Introduction to Modern Information Retrieval, which outlined a simple automatic indexing process (page 71, passim):

  • Identify the individual words that constitute the document  

    • One finds words by parsing text, i.e., you break text on the presence of white space. It's not too difficult to program a computer to read in a line of text and split it apart into "words" based on the location of white space between "words."

    For example, the phrase brothers-in-law's banquet becomes two words: brothers-in-law's and banquet.

    Usually, some normalization process is then used to take care of internal punctuation. For example, all internal punctuation might be elided making brothers-in-law's into the word brothersinlaws. Alternatively, you might program your normalization process to further reduce words based on their internal punctuation. In this case, brothers-in-law's would become the words: (1) brothers, (2) in, (3) law and (4) s.

    The latter method of normalization is more common than the former. The reason is that few of your users will recognize "brothersinlaws" as a legitimate query term, but they can be taught to deal with "s" as a word.

    Big philosophical question: "s" is a word?

    Consequently, the phrase brothers-in-law's banquet might be considered (depending on the exact parsing and normalization routines) either two, three, four, or five words. It all depends.

  • Eliminate high frequency words (i.e., stopwords)

    • Years ago, when computer memory was not so cheap and abundant, one had to figure out ways to avoid bulking your computer with "useless", "non-significant" words that didn't carry information, i.e., in the English language, 'the' is an article that appears very frequently but carries little unique information.

    Stopwords are words that are stopped from being placed in an index. There are many lists of stopwords, but commonly English words such as "the", "a", "an", "and", "or", "not" are not indexed.

    Did you know that Le Thé is tea in French and that The is a common last name? What would Alexander say if you told him that his last name didn't have any useful information? Wouldn't that hurt his feelings? What if he told you that he didn't think your last name was so hot? Would this promote world peace? etc., etc.


  • Stem the remaining words

    • Stemming is the process of reducing word forms down to a simpler radical.

    Salton explains: "It is useful first to remove word suffixes (and possibly also prefixes), thereby reducing the original words to word stem form. This reduces a variety of different forms such as analysis, analyzing, analyzer, analyzed, and analysing to the common word stem 'analy'."

    Picky question: Is "analyzer" the same as "analysis"? What if you were looking for "analyzer" but were given a lot of documents about "analysis?" This would make you happy?


  • Weight the index terms by their frequency of occurrence in documents.

    • Perhaps one of Salton's most significant contributions was the idea recognizing that words don't occur randomly in documents, but depending on the topic, some words occur more frequently than others. Therefore, these words should be given more weight in the index, i.e., if the word "dog" occurred 6 times in a document, then that document was pretty "doggy" compared to a document where "dog" occurred only once.

    Building an index with weighted terms means that you can deliver the documents to the searcher in rank order by their "dogginess," i.e., give a patron looking for "dog" the document with six "dogs" before you give him the document with only one "dog."

    Perhaps an example would better illustrate this effect. Consider the three documents below and determine which one is "doggiest", that is, the sort of document that someone searching for "dog" would want to see first. Rank these documents by counting the times that "dog" and its variants occur.

    Is the high ranking document the "doggiest"?


    My dog is named Spot.
    "Dog" occurs once.
    I saw two dogs on the way to school. One dog was named Spot and the other dog bit me.
    "Dog" occurs 3 times.
    "Fishing for Dog Fish" by Cameron Dog. Dog fish gaffs that feature a left dog leg handle are most useful in securing dog fish.

Politics, Religion, Food Fights, etc., in Information Retrieval

Information retrieval is quite controversial. It attempts to index the meaning of documents via a mechanical processing of language. It is easy to be skeptical, but something has to be done with the torrent of digital documents, or else we'll have to invent Google (yikes!).
Some folks have tried linguistic methods such as natural language processing and other folks have tried fuzzy logic. Believe me, if something really worked somebody would be very famous and wealthy.

Heirs of Salton

The vision of automatically 'understanding' text is simply too attractive (necessary?) to go away. The Web is simply too big and too rich an information resource to be handled manually and ignored. There must be some way to automatically understand the Web and harvest meaning from it!

Professor Oren Etzioni, University of Washington, has been working on the KnowItAll project.

"Using search engines to compile a list - like the top 50 greatest blues guitarists by record sales, say - involves a lot of drudge work because you have to visit many web pages to gather the data you need. But the next step in search engine technology could make creating such lists possible with a single mouse click.
KnowItAll, a search engine under development at the University of Washington, Seattle, trawls the web for data and then collates it in the form of a list. The approach is unique, says its developer, Oren Etzioni, because it generates information that probably does not exist on any single web page.
Etzioni's ultimate aim is to have KnowItAll answer questions such as "list all British scientists born before 1900". The software cannot do that yet, because it lacks a module that can understand "natural-language" questions of this type. That will come later, he says.
