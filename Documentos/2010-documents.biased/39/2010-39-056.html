Welcoming winter

Doug Ramsay photo

Lora Burke, 10, of Snohomish re-lights one of 500 paper bag lanterns along the Riverfront Trail during Snohomish’s celebration of winter solstice Dec. 21. Unlike last year’s snowy solstice, this year’s event was slightly warmer and wet as rain and wind required the re-lighting of many of the lanterns that stretched from Cady Park to Avenue D.

Thank you, volunteers

Sky Valley Food Bank volunteers Kristi Higgins, Sonia Ohlde and Kathy Challancin. The volunteers at the Sky Valley Food Bank in Monroe worked hundreds of hours preparing and distributing food, holiday food and toys. The three volunteers pictured helped distribute toys through the food bank’s Precious Toys program. More than 570 families came for help and more than 2,800 individuals received food over the holiday season. More than 700 children received nice, new toys and stockings stuffed to the brim with goodies. Many anonymous women knitted sweaters, hats and scarves for children. And several clubs and businesses donated money and services. The food bank thanks all the people who helped their fellow community members in their time of need. Donations are always welcome and can be received through the mail at PO Box 724, Monroe, WA 98272 or through the Web site at www.svfoodbank.org. For more information, call 360-794-7959.

Additional time to review airport study
EVERETT - Are you one of the residents who are concerned about commercial passenger service at Paine Field but won’t have the time in the next two weeks to get through the 86-page report explaining how it would affect you?
You’re in luck. Residents now have until the end of January to comment on the recently-released Paine Field study, which analyzes how commercial passenger service would affect the surrounding area.
Snohomish County released the draft environmental report earlier this month after two airlines asked to start passenger flights from the airport last year. Horizon Airlines has said it would operate four times per day to Portland and twice per day to Spokane using 75-seat airplanes, and Allegiant would operate twice a week to Las Vegas using 150-seat jets.
Residents and elected officials alike have come out against commercial passenger service at the airport along the eastern border of Mukilteo, but federal law obligates the county to accommodate airline proposals before rejecting them.
Residents who oppose passenger service, including Save Our Communities, the leading activist group, say there are too many residential neighborhoods near Paine Field to accommodate the traffic and noise airport expansion could bring. According to the report, Paine Field is surrounded by single-family, suburban homes, especially west of Paine Field Boulevard.
Overall, the report addresses residents’ major concerns, stating that passenger service would not drastically increase noise levels, clog the roads or pollute the air.
Noise
While more airplanes will mean more noise, the increases are not significant, and levels will not increase in a way that is harmful to nearby residences, according to the report.
Computer models helped county researchers estimate future noise levels, which were measured at all times of day. Any noise that came from operations occurring after 10 p.m. and before 7 a.m. was given a 10 decibel penalty because of the “higher sensitivity to noise at night,” according to the report.
Noise levels did not exceed the threshold the Federal Aviation Administration sets for “noise-sensitive land uses,” such as residences, schools, churches and hospitals.
There are small areas where noise level increases extend off airport property, but in those cases, the land is used for commercial or industrial purposes and is “considered compatible with this level of aircraft noise,” according to the report.
Traffic
Passenger service will not cause Snohomish County arterials or any Mukilteo or Everett intersections to change from “acceptable” to “deficient” levels of service, according to the report.
The project will add trips to three intersections that are anticipated to have “deficient” levels of service with or without the airport expansion. These intersections are state Route 99 at Airport Road in Everett, the I-5 southbound ramp at 128th Street SW and the I-5 northbound ramp at 128th Street SW.
New terminal
Bringing passenger service to Paine Field will mean building a new $3 million terminal at the airport.
The terminal would have two boarding gates accommodating 225 people, additional parking and a covered breezeway, translating to 29,000 square feet in additional space.
Construction is estimated to be complete in the summer of 2010.
Because expansion includes a new terminal, the report includes reports to the Stillaquamish, Tulalip and Sauk-Suiattle tribes, stating the project will not affect historic properties.
The report also includes a report on threatened and endangered species. Only two endangered species have been spotted at Paine Field, the Bald Eagle and the Peregrine Falcon. The falcon was seen once in 2004, and eagles are seen infrequently. Neither makes its home at the airport, so the development would not negatively impact the animals.
Commercial passenger service would cause an increase in the emissions of pollutants into the air, according to the report, but increases were expected to increase anyway because activity levels at the airport are expected to increase. Air pollutant levels would not increase in a significant way by adding passenger service planes to the airport.
Citizen comments
The report is available online at http://www.painefield.com/airserviceea.html or at local libraries. Residents have three ways to comment on the environmental assessment: by e-mail, mail or at a public hearing.
E-mail comments to airserviceeacomments@snoco.org, or mail them to Dave Waggoner, Director, Snohomish County Airport, 3220 100th St. SW., Suite A, Everett, WA 98204.
Comments must be received by Jan. 29.
Residents can also testify at two public hearings, where a stenographer will transcribe comments and return them to the Federal Aviation Administration.
The public hearings are from 6 p.m. to 9 p.m., on Jan. 4 at Meadowdale High School in Lynnwood, and Jan. 5 at the Snohomish PUD Auditorium in Everett.
