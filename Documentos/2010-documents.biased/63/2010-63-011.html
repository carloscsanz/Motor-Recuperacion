    Google gives publishers more control over news index

    By Kenneth Corbin on December 2, 2009 7:56 PM

    Amid mounting criticism from at least one outspoken newspaper publisher, Google said today that it would offer news Web sites more control over how their content is indexed and made available through Google News.

    That one publisher, of course, is News Corp. boss Rupert Murdoch, who as recently as yesterday blasted the practice of online aggregation, calling it theft and vowing to begin setting up paywalls for his newspapers' Web sites.

    Murdoch had previously threatened to pull News Corp. content from Google's search engine.

    Well, many people didn't take his bluster seriously, but just in case, Google has made it a little easier for him if that's the way he wants to go.

    Sites had always had the option to keep themselves out of Google's index by using the universally accepted protocol known as robots.txt -- simple code that give instructions to Web crawlers about how much, if any, of the site they are permitted to index.

    Google has now deployed a separate Web crawler for its News site, which will streamline the mechanism for keeping news content off limits. (Google had previously offered an opt out for its News site, but it required publishers to submit a contact form in Google's help center.)

    Now, that process will be automated, so Webmasters will be able to automatically set instructions for the crawler to leave all or parts of their content off the site.

    The additional controls will also allow news publishers to keep their content available on regular Google searches, but remove it from the News site.

    As the grousing from Murdoch and others has grown louder, Google has consistently said that publishers are welcome to remove their content, but in the same breath reminded them that its sites send billions of clicks to their sites, traffic that they otherwise might not have seen, and could of course be monetized.

    Josh Cohen, Google's senior business product manager, put it tactfully on the company's blog:

    "Most people put their content on the web because they want it to be found, so very few choose to exclude their material from Google. But we respect publishers' wishes. If publishers don't want their websites to appear in web search results or in Google News, we want to give them easy ways to remove it."
