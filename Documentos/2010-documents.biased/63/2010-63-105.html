Using the Weather to Cool Data Centers

By KATE GALBRAITH

The Associated Press The climate of places like Buffalo make them efficient locales for data centers, which have a need to keep cool.

Can nature — in the form of the famously chilly Irish winds, or the brisk air of Buffalo — help to keep data storage centers cool?

Microsoft, Yahoo and other data powerhouses apparently think so. Last month, Microsoft opened a data storage center in Dublin, which it said would take advantage of “the naturally colder Ireland climate to cool the facility,” resulting in greater efficiencies.

In June, Yahoo announced that it would locate a data center in Buffalo, to take advantage of the “micro-climate,” to cool the servers entirely with outside air — suggesting that, at least as it relates to data centers, the Cold Belt has a competitive advantage over sunnier climes.

And Google has a data center in Belgium where, according to Niki Fenwick, a spokeswoman, “the local climate allows us to efficiently cool the data center without needing to use electricity to power chillers.”

She noted, however, that “not all Google data centers can be located in cold climates, because we want our tools to be as fast as possible.”

In other words, the transmission of data can slow down over long distances.

Data centers use vast amounts of energy. In 2006, they accounted for 1.5 percent of electricity use in the United States, or more than the annual usage of the state of Massachusetts. And their electricity consumption is projected to grow at about 12 percent a year.

In many cases, according to Yahoo’s blog, half of the energy usage is dedicated to keeping the servers cool to maximize the efficiency of the chips that run them.

“Cool” is a relative term, of course. Typically, data centers need to stay below 80 degrees, according to Andres Carvallo, the chief information officer of Austin Energy, a utility in the heart of Texas’s high-tech “Silicon Hills.”

Texas, of course, is not competing to attract cold-weather data centers, Mr. Carvallo noted. In general, he said, “There’s certainly a renaissance around designing a data center itself.”

Many data centers, he explained, have traditionally been designed “like a vault” — with no windows or access to the outside air. That is changing. There is even an effort on the island nation of Mauritius to cool data centers with seawater piped in from offshore.

Many companies, including Microsoft and Yahoo, are also locating their data centers near hydroelectric plants, allowing them to play up the virtues of renewable power.

There is, however, a bottom-line reason, too, Mr. Carvallo said. Hydro power is generally less -expensive than power from other sources — an important selling point for owners of the power-hungry centers.
