Gr8ful's Full Review: VisionTek Xtasy 9550, (128 MB) AGP Video Card
I recently received a replacement computer from my insurance company for one damaged in a basement flood a couple months ago. The old computer was a Compaq 5050 desktop PC from 1998 which had a Celeron 333 processor and a miniscule hard drive. Needless to say I didn’t use it for much other than a firewall for my LAN. I have already written an article on the replacement PC, which was a brand new Compaq SR1550NX .

What I found was the computer was great for everything except gaming since the onboard graphics left a lot to be desired and borrowed memory from the main system RAM. I decided to get an accelerator card for it and did not want to dump a lot of cash on it because I already have two gaming systems that have decent graphics cards in them and this PC will be mostly used for music and video editing. I looked around at a couple places and found the XTASY ATI Radeon 9550 for $79.99 at Best Buy. It was inexpensive compared to most of the other cards I had looked at and the specs on the box looked promising so I coughed up the dough an took it home.


The Radeon 9550

The card has some nice features and is fast enough to play CounterStrike Source with 12 or less total players. More than that will cause some video glitches and rendering lag. I have also found that toning down the game settings a little bit goes a long way to making the card run the game much smoother. This video card handles most current games decent enough but some games like Doom3 and Battlefield 2 will tax it to its limits. Surprisingly, it did ok with HL2 with only some minor slow frames during the most intense scenes.

Quick Features
* Full HLSL and GLSlang support
* 250MHz VPU Core
* Memory 400MHz DDR (200MHz Dual chanel)
* 2 High speed geometry engines
* Improved Z caching
* Multiple rendering targets
* TRUFORM II High Order Surfaces
* Multisample AA modes
* Anisotropic filtering


While these features look impressive, some of them must be disabled in order to play games with demanding system requirements. TRUFORM II is virtually useless on this card unless you are playing single player games from two years ago. Anisotropic filtering must be disabled in almost any game unless you like low frame rates and don’t even get me started with the Anti-Aliasing. Even at 2x Anti-Aliasing slows the frame rates by more than 15FPS on most games. As for the improved Z buffer caching… I can’t see a difference from the older Radeon 8500, 9200 or even the 9000.

The truth is this card will play modern games “ok” but you need to disable all the bells and whistles if you want to play in 1024 x 768 resolution. It isn’t a bad card, just a cheap one.

Real Time Performance

Ok, enough with the technical jargon, here is what it boils down to. ATI has packed a lot of features into this card but most of these are just there so they can say the card is compatible with modern games. And it is… but, you will find that most games play but aren’t very enjoyable unless you turn off many of these so called features, especially Anisotropic filtering and Anti-Aliasing. These two features are used to make the 3D image smoother and “prettier” to look at but at the sacrifice of frame rates. Any self respecting gamer would rather have a smooth game play than “pretty” graphics. The real gamers don’t settle for one or the other and that is what they make the more expensive cards for. The Radeon 9550 can play the same games as the Radeon X850 but you will have to sacrifice either smoothness of frame rate or turn off most of the extra features. In some cases with more demanding games, you will have slow frame rates with this card regardless of how many features you disable.


My experience

As I said in the beginning, this computer is mainly used for music and video editing. The only reason I even put a graphics card in it is so my friends can play on the LAN when they come over. I now have four computers set up for gaming in my den and room for people to bring their own when I have LAN parties. If my friends want a nice smooth gaming experience with all the niceties of the more expensive cards, well… they can just bring their own PC over and hook into the LAN.

I have played CounterStrike Source, HL2 and Battlefield 2 on this computer with the Radeon 9550 and after turning off most of the features, I got decent frame rates… good enough to play without being able to blame frame rates for getting killed. I did enjoy playing using the 9550, but I soon found myself back at my personal machine with my X800.


Benchmarks

3DMark 2001SE
Total 3DMarks 7277
The card performed similarly to my Radeon 8500 on a much slower CPU. The 9550 seemed to do better in the high detail game benchmarks but slower in the low details, however it did not perform well at all with Fill Rate and High Polygon counts. I will refrain from adding the total benchmark to this article, but it did prove that the card has some worth and is capable of hanging with the big dogs. The most surprising results were the Environment Bump Mapping and the Pixel Shader tests coming in at 112FPS and 95FPS respectively. Keep in mind that when you are playing a game, anything over 40FPS is acceptable but over 50 is preferable. To give you some perspective on the score, top end video cards are benchmarking between 25000 and 30000 in 3DMark 2001SE.


3DMark03 v1.6.0
3DMark Score 1807
CPU Score 809

3DMark03 runs a 3D benchmark a little different than 3DMark 2001SE, it also scores the CPU for 3D abilities. The CPU in this case is an Athlon 64 3400+ running at 2.41 GHz and that does make a difference in the total 3D score as well. If you use a slower CPU, your 3DMark score will be lower as well as the CPU score. Taking that into account I noticed again that the graphics card did not perform well with Fill Rates and with a lower FPS than in the previous benchmark. It did however compare well to other systems with similar setups and the same driver version. Its strongest points were made in the shaders while its weakest points were lost in Fill Rate and overall frame rate. Keep in mind also that this benchmark program is very intensive and most games do not stress a video card anywhere near where this benchmark does.

Again to give some perspective on the score, top end cards are benching between 6000 and 10000 in 3DMark03. But to give some perspective to actual real time gaming, I was getting an average of 48FPS in CounterStrike Source and an average of 43FPS in Half Life 2. Battlefield 2 on the other hand I averaged only 29FPS with short bursts up into the 40’s. This is still adequate to play the game but it’s not as easy to aim or even focus on distant objects when the frame rate drops below 40FPS.

I have not taken the time to download 3DMark05 yet and I may not get around to it for a while but these benchmarks show enough to decide if the card will perform good enough for what you want to do.


Conclusion

This card will suffice for light gaming and people who do not intend to spend more than $100 for a graphics card. If you are a hard core gamer this is not the card for you. People who game as a pass-time will enjoy the card well enough but people who game for status and glory (a little overkill?) will most likely hate the card. Personally, I prefer my X800 but I did not find the card atrociously inadequate, in fact as I stated earlier, it was enjoyable to play games with the Radeon 9550, just not as enjoyable as my X800.
