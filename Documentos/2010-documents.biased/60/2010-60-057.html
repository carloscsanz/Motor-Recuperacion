Feel The GeForce
We Test Five Fiery Graphics Cards
Many people use computers for simple, every-day tasks: checking e-mail, surfing the Web, writing a letter, or figuring finances. And then there are those who love eye-candy, lots of it, and the sweeter the better. Fill our monitors with 3-D games and their sparkly colors, rich detail, and amazing textures because we can't get enough.

Great graphics don't appear out of nowhere. Processing graphical data requires intense number crunching, and nothing churns through geometric data like an NVIDIA GeForce graphics chip. The GeForce graphics chip has two flavors: the GeForce 256 and the newer, and supposedly faster, GeForce2 GTS. We gathered five graphics cards incorporating GeForce chips (three with 256 and two GTS) and put them through rigorous and thoroughly uninteresting tests. OK, mostly we played games, but we also determined which graphics card would put the biggest smile on your face.

How We Tested

We tested the graphics cards on a 450MHz Pentium III system with 128MB of SDRAM. We used three tests to benchmark the performance: First, we measured how well it handles 2-D applications using MadOnion's SYSmark 2000 test. This benchmark is divided into an Office Productivity score and a Content Creation score, which are combined into an Overall score. Office Productivity measures how well the graphics card runs standard office software, such as Microsoft Word, and Content Creation measures the card's ability to run programs, such as Adobe's PhotoShop.

Next we measured sustained frame rate using Quake III, a popular 3-D game, while running in OpenGL mode. OpenGL is a powerful API (application program interface) designed to enhance 3-D graphics. A graphics card needs a frame rate of 30fps (frames per second) to keep images and video streams from looking choppy. Our last test was the MadOnion 3DMark2000 test, which further measures a graphics card's 3-D ability. The higher the score, the better the card processes 3-D data.

Creative Labs 3D Blaster Annihilator Pro
$245
Creative Labs
(800) 998-1000, (408) 428-6600
http://www.americas.creative.com/home.html

The first graphics card we'll dissect is the 3D Blaster Annihilator Pro from Creative Labs. This graphics card is based on the GeForce 256 graphics processor.

The Annihilator Pro is an AGP (accelerated graphics port) 2X/4X graphics card with 32MB of speedy DDR memory. It has a powerful 350MHz RAMDAC (RAM digital-to-analog converter), which is important because this is where all of those ones and zeros get turned into the image you see on your monitor. The maximum resolution for this graphics card is 2,048 x 1,536, and refresh rates range from 60Hz to 240Hz. The Annihilator Pro has the typical 32-bit maximum color depth.

The Annihilator Pro supports Open-GL and DirectX APIs and Windows 95/98 (Win9x), Windows NT 4.0 and Windows 2000.

Our test results showed that the Annihilator Pro did not annihilate the competition in this review, although it performed well enough. We ran the SYSmark 2000 benchmark first. The SYSmark scores were similar among the graphics cards we tested, although the Annihilator Pro finished last or tied for last in all three categories. It posted an Overall score of 90, an Office Productivity score of 87, and a Content Creation score of 93. None are bad scores, although they were all slightly lower than other cards we tested.

The Annihilator Pro fared about the same during our Quake III test. It produced acceptable test results but lagged behind the other graphics cards we tested. The card reached 57.6fps at a resolution of 800 x 600, which is certainly well above the 30fps minimum you'll need for smooth video. The frame rate dropped a bit when we increased the resolution to 1,024 x 768, to 52.5fps. Both scores are acceptable, although they were both the lowest among graphic cards we tested.

We finished testing the Annihilator Pro with the 3DMark2000 test. This is the only test in which this graphics card did not finish last, posting a respectable score of 3,685 in the 3DMark2000 test, enough to finish in the middle of the five cards we reviewed.

Elsa Erazor X2
$299
Elsa
(877) 819-8570,
(408) 961-4600
http://www.elsa.com

Elsa may not be on the tip of your tongue when it comes to naming top graphics card manufacturers, but that will change if you ever get a firsthand look at the Erazor X2. In fact, the Erazor X2 is our Smart Choice for the graphic cards in this roundup.

The Erazor X2 is based on a GeForce 256 graphics processor. The X2 is an AGP 2X/4X graphic card with 32MB of DDR memory. The refresh rates for the X2 range from 60Hz to 200Hz. The maximum resolution is 1,900 x 1,440, which should be sufficient even though it's lower than the maximum resolutions of the other graphics cards we tested. The graphics card also has the typical 32-bit color depth, and a 350MHz RAMDAC. It's compatible with the OpenGL and DirectX APIs, of course, and runs on the Win9x, WinNT 4.0, and Win2000 operating systems.

The results from the first test were just above average. It finished first in all three categories, but just barely. The X2 had an overall score of 92, an Office Productivity score of 92, and a Content Creation score of 95.

Next we fired up Quake III to check out the Erazor X2's frame rate. The card did quite well here. The frame rate was 65.2fps at a resolution of 800 x 600, which is faster than anything else we looked at. The frame rate took a hit when we kicked the resolution up to 1,024 x 768, dropping to 55.7fps. That was only good enough for fourth place, although 55.7fps is still pretty impressive.

Our final test was the 3D-Mark2000 benchmark, and the X2 made a good showing here. The overall 3DMark-2000 score was 3,719, which is better than anything else we've tested. This clearly indicates that the X2 knows how to handle 3-D applications.

Guillemot Hercules Prophet II GTS
$359
Guillemot
(877) 484-5536, (514) 279-9960
http://www.hercules.com

Guillemot snatched up Hercules, the venerable graphics card maker, late last year. The Hercules brand is back with some powerful new graphic cards, including the Prophet II GTS, based on NVIDIA's GeForce2 GTS graphics processor. The Hercules Prophet II GTS is a 4X/2X AGP graphics card that has 32MB of DDR RAM. The graphic card's refresh rates range from 60Hz to 240Hz. The Prophet II has a maximum resolution of 2,048 x 1,536 and the typical 32-bit color depth. The graphics card also has the typical powerful 350MHz RAMDAC. It supports both the OpenGL and Direct3D APIs. It's compatible with the Win9x and Win2000 operating systems.

The Prophet II didn't blow away our SYSmark test, but it performed just above average compared to the other graphics cards. The Overall score was 91, with an Office Productivity score of 89 and a Content Creation score of 93. These scores are OK, but certainly not dramatically better than any other graphic card we've tested.

The Prophet II was quite impressive in the Quake III test, however. The images appeared fast and smooth, and the test results bear that out. The Prophet II posted a frame rate of 64.2fps at a resolution of 800 x 600, second only to the Elsa Erazor X2. The Prophet II sustained a frame rate of 59.9fps when we kicked the resolution up to 1,024 x 768. This score also was the second fastest. Leadtek's GTS graphics card leap-frogged the Prophet II while the Erazor X2 dropped to fourth place in this test. We were quite impressed with this graphic card's 3-D power.

The Prophet II proved to be relatively weak in our 3DMark 2000 test, which surprised us. The overall score was 3,424, which was second to last among graphics cards in this roundup. We blame the fill rate (the speed at which the graphics card can create texels, which are the smallest unit of a texture map, similar to a pixel in an image), which was rather slow for this card.

Leadtek WinFast GeForce 256 DDR
$240
Leadtek
(510) 490-8076
http://www.leadtek.com

Leadtek was kind enough to send us two entries for this review. We'll look at the WinFast GeForce 256 DDR graphics card first. This one is based on the (you guessed it!) GeForce 256 graphics processor.

The WinFast 256 is an AGP 2X/4X graphics card with 32MB of DDR RAM. It has a 350MHz RAMDAC, like the other graphics cards we tested, and has a maximum resolution of 2,048 x 1,536. The available refresh rates are from 60Hz to 240Hz, and the graphics card has a maximum 32-bit color depth. The WinFast 256 works with the OpenGL and DirectX APIs, as well as with the Win9x, WinNT 4.0, and Win2000 operating systems.

Our tests indicate that the WinFast 256 graphic card is a solid choice when it comes to 2-D graphics acceleration, but it isn't as strong at 3-D as some of the other graphics cards in this review. The SYSmark 2000 test went quite well. The WinFast 256 had an Overall score of 91, just one point shy of the Elsa Erazor X2's high mark. The WinFast 256 registered an Office Productivity score of 88 and a Content Creation score of 94, both very solid scores.

The WinFast 256 wasn't as robust in our 3-D tests, however. In the Quake III test, the card only managed to create 60.3fps at a resolution of 800 x 600. The frame rate dropped only slightly when we increased the resolution to 1,024 x 768, to 58.9fps. That's actually not too bad.

What was bad was the WinFast 256's 3DMark2000 score of 3,399, the lowest among cards we tested. This proves that the Leadtek 256 can't hang with the other graphics cards we tested when it comes to 3-D power.

Leadtek WinFast GeForce2 GTS
$340
Leadtek
(510) 490-8076
http://www.leadtek.com

The other graphics card from Leadtek was the WinFast GeForce2 GTS graphic card. This card stumbled in our 2-D tests, but was great in the 3-D tests.

The specifications for the WinFast GeForce2 GTS are very much like those of the other cards in this review. It is an AGP 2X/4X graphics card with 32MB of DDR memory and a 350MHz RAMDAC. Its maximum resolution is 2,048 x 1,536 and available refresh rates range from 60Hz to 240Hz. It has the standard 32-bit color depth. The WinFast GTS is compatible with the OpenGL and DirectX APIs, and it runs on Win9x, WinNT 4.0, and Win2000 operating systems.

The WinFast GTS's SYSmark2000 scores show that it is slightly behind the pack when it comes to accelerating 2-D applications. The WinFast GTS's Overall score was 90, which tied the Annihilator Pro for the worst Overall score; however, this is still only two points lower than the Erazor X2's Overall score of 92, which finished first. The Office Productivity score was 87, also tied for last. The Content Creation score was 94.

The WinFast GTS really showed its stripes during the next series of tests. It posted 63.5fps at a resolution of 800 x 600 during the Quake III trial. The number barely dropped when we increased the resolution to 1,024 x 768. It sustained a frame rate of 60.1.

The WinFast GTS scored very well in our 3DMark2000 test, too. The card's overall score was 3,706. The WinFast GTS was barely edged out for the highest 3DMark2000 score by the Elsa Erazor X2's score of 3,719.

Final Word

It didn't take long to determine which product would be our Smart Choice recipient. The Elsa Erazor X2 was the perfect combination of performance and value. Our testing indicates that there's not a significant different in performance between a NVIDIA 256 graphics chip and a NVIDIA GTS chip. That being the case, you're probably better off getting a graphics card with the cheaper GeForce 256 graphics chip. There are several other graphics cards making their way to the market based on the GeForce2 GTS chip, however, so we may yet come across a GTS-based card that performs well enough to justify its higher price.

by Michael Sweet


Buying Tips


A graphics card with TV/Video Out lets you play games on your television.

Check for the latest drivers as soon as you install your graphics card.

Your graphics card is only as good as your monitor. If your card supports 1,900 x 1,440 in 32-bit color with a 150Hz (hertz) refresh rate, but your monitor can't, you won't be able to use that setting.

Some graphics cards may offer 64MB (megabytes) of built-in memory, but 32MB of memory should be more than enough.

Graphics cards with DDR (Double Data Rate) memory are faster than graphics cards with regular single data rate SDRAM (synchronous dynamic random-access memory) memory.
