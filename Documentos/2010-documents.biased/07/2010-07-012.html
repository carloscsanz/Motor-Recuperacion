Yeah, the margin of error is one thing that makes it difficult. Another thing that made it diffiucult was the fact that there was often a southerly crosswind that came down off the mountains to the north, so one of the tactics in that case was to make a more wide-angle approach so that you weren't pushed over the harbor by the time you were lined up on rnwy 13. When the wind was kicking up (quite often), landings didn't look pretty and you were always kicking rudder at the last moment to line up with the runway instead landing in the harbor.

Another complicating factor was that mountain range that ran east-west just north of Kai Tak. This mountain range divided Hong Kong into its Kowloon side and its New Territories side (with Sha Tin city just on the other side of the mountain....used to be a small village when I was a kid there where my grandparents lived at one time....or more correctly, in Dai Moh San).

So, that meant that planes couldn't just make a straight in ILS approach to rnwy 13 because the runway pointed toward the west side of Kowloon's mountain range. So, planes flew in at an angle, heading towards the middle to eastern side of the mountains and homed in on a signal on top of a butte/knoll called "checkerboard hill" all the while waiting for rnwy 13's IGS (inertial guidance system) signal to center on their instruments telling them it was time to bank and head toward the runway. The bank angle (depending on when you actually decided to turn...a bit later if you had that nearly perpendicular crosswind) was upwards of 47 degrees. A tad higher if you banked later. That meant a significant right-bank turn, while descending and figuring in crosswind, and you've got the buildings/streets rising up on you pretty fast. This was quite hairy if relying more on instruments. Hong Kong can get kinda cloudy/foggy over that area. It was a bit "easier" if you had a clear line of sight to the runway while making that turn, but that wasn't always in your favor. Then you had kids like me shooting the planes flying overhead with their "slingshots".

The Cathay Pacific (HKG's airline) pilots always made it look easy because they flew it day in and day out.

I'm not sure that you're really saying anything different that what I was describing. Not only my uncle who flew for Cathay Pacific but other pilots I met in HKG said that their banks were often at about 45-47degrees in order to stay visually lined up with the runway once the offset correction point was arrived at. Yes, the bank angle is technically 47degrees because of the ILS/IGS offset. (I believe the Macau airport's (VMMC) southerly landing approach is an IGS offset too because of mountains but is a left bank.) Banks were sometimes a tad greater depending on wind, so a bit more correction at the last minute for landing, albeit a rougher/scarier-looking landing for passengers. So I think we're basically describing the same thing....yours from an official technical brochure of the procedure, my report from the cockpit experience.

If you stood a little higher up on the mountain side behind checkerboard hill on a clear day to see planes approach, you'd see them round the northside of Laan Tau island (where VHHH is now), head toward Lion Rock Mountain (more or less) over Stone-Cutters island (which is basically no more), pass just to the south of the Mei Foo city and container-port area, and heading toward Kowloon Tong area where the Checkerboard is situated just a little more to the east of Lion Rock. As the plane would pass over the Kowloon Jai soccer field at the base of the Checkerboard, they'd be into their turn toward the airport. (Covering your ears was often necessary...I used to play tennis at the courts right next to the soccer field.) Visual cues on the ground often meant the approach lights...critical in low-light situations, for sure, but so were other visible condition cues often passed around by some of the pilots such as the park and flying until "Lion Rock" filled your windscreen (a bit more of a "seat of your pants" description, but most who flew this approach knew that that meant even if the AIP didn't say that).

Most companies followed the AIP of course, but had their own unique "style" or company procedure for nailing the landing as best as possible. For a time, the Korean planes seemed to make some pretty scary "fighter" approaches, at least the ones I used to watch. They were a bit faster it seemed, banked later and thus steeper giving their approaches more of a "nose-down" look. I think there are some photos of Kai Tak approaches made by Korean Airline 747 pilots that look positively dangerous. I often saw pilots banking late and slightly below DH, other times banking too early and a bit higher than the DH....both styles made for some interesting spotting as you could see them make various last minute and often stressful corrections to get lined up on 13 when the wind was blowing.

All said and done, I don't think you were being a "smart ass". You might have mistook my description of "flying in at an angle" for something other than the way the planes would make their straight and level approach in relation to arriving over Kowloon, where their heading was in relation to the mountains/Checkerboard, where runway was lined up, and then the 47degree bank when it was time to get or stay centered on the IGS. At an angle meant the direction they were heading all the while keeping Kai Tak in sight just off the to right in their constand descent over the city.
