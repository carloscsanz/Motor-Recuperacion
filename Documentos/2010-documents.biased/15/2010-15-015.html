Benchmark Poll–Should they standardize?

Oct. 12, 2001 (11:39 am) By: Rick Hodgin

With all the confusion recently among the various CPU manufacturers and the relative performance levels of their product lines, is it time to ask a serious question? Should a standard benchmark be created and used by all manufacturers? It seems like a no-brainer! Obviously, to compare performance a standard benchmark is needed. This allows other factors to be considered, such as peripheral equipment, additional software, and overall value of the total package. Without a standard benchmark, another variable is introduced which serves only to confuse the consumer. Please visit The Inquirer and participate in their poll.
Rob Note: One important factor is that the “standard benchmark” must be Open Source. Otherwise we are back at square one and can't really feel a sense of “trust” towards a blackbox benchmark if AMD or Intel suddenly starts scoring much higher (or lower) with a new chip. Van of Van's Hardware has been pushing for an Open Source benchmark, but I don't know if anything has come of that.

Hmmm… but what about stability? (2:58pm EST Fri Oct 12 2001)
For the longest time, the SPEC benchmarks were used, as they were pretty good computational measures and they were very standardized. With the advent of superoptimizing compilers though, one compile of SPECINT97 could vary by as much as 40% over another, on a given machine. Clearly something wrong with “open source” at some level.

I think Intel was onto something with their iSpec initiative a few years ago, where they used a “composite” benchmark of 40% realtime, 30% office productivity, 20% scientific computation and 10% something else of measured runtimes of programs released no more than 3 months behind their chip technology. This gave a way for the benchmark apps to have their SSE's (generically) enabled, so as to provide new and better numbers.

But, it was also selfserving to Intel: they paid vast amounts of money to ensure that THEIR optimizations were compiled into THEIR in-the-pocket commercial application producer's products. So, it went kerblaat.

I'd be happy with a composite “benchie” that does the same thing as Intel's, except at a micro level: 40% of instructions from cache, 30% using CPU specific extension instructions, 20% based on “biggish” problems exceeding cache by 10x, and 10% related to I/O, busses, and the like.

Wouldn't that essentially eliminate the “spin” and effect of aggressive investment by one producer to achieve specific results?

On the other hand, it could just be a goatdream.

Well, they're not really “standards” (7:20pm EST Sat Oct 13 2001)
The only “standards” around so far are PC1600 and PC2100. PC2700/DDR333 is lurking in the weeds, but not finalized. As long as DDR-makers avoid compatibility problems by running at the same V(rimm), I don't see that there's any problem. For example, “PC2400″ (not a standard) is just DDR266 that will run faster, and that's the way DDR333 (not yet a standard) will probably be defined as well.

Either PC2400 or DDR333 will run just fine as DDR266 – or even DDR200, for that matter. (It might get bored!)

The main thing will be for mobos to be able to handle the change, which will include their being able to maintain the other bus frequencies within some tolerable distance of their “standard” value. The new IWill DDR333 board addresses that problem by having both 1/5 and 1/6 dividers built in, so it will be able to provide an accurate 33MHz bus at DDR333 and even DDR400 fsb speeds.
