A. Report on Library Project

            My project was to organize two neglected book display shelves in the main branch of the New Haven Public Library on Elm Street. The books on the display shelves were supposed to feature African American books.  The project was two-fold: To fill in the display with current titles and to make the display attractive and attention-getting.

            When I first sought to work on a library project, I went to the branch I usually visit, which is the Stetson Branch on Dixwell Avenue. There, I talked to Diane Brown-Pettaway, who was eager to help me find a meaningful project and advised me that I would do better to come downtown, where she normally worked as the Youth Librarian for the city. Because of her position, she stressed that the particular book cases she needed improved would also need to feature young adult reference books for students working on book reports and history projects.

            At this point I thought that there would not be enough room for the young adult books and the books that I was interested in putting up front. I took it for granted that the main branch already had current titles and it would just be a matter of moving them from the stacks. I was wrong.

            The biggest part of the project turned out to be compiling lists of books that needed to be ordered! By browsing the pages of Black Issues Book Review Magazine, I came up with a list of books and gave it to Dale Johnson, who is the Supervising Librarian at the main branch. She was very receptive to my ideas and ordered most of the books on the list.

            Still thinking that there was only a small amount of space to work with, I had envisioned a system which would employ monthly themes. Some of the books would be returned to the stacks and more books would be brought forward. For example, April is Poetry Month, so collections of African American Poetry would be placed on the main display shelf for the month of April.

            Once I began to actually place books on the shelves, I found that there was a lot more to fill up than I first imagined. I was able to maintain the area for young adult reference, biographies, and other non-fiction for the students in one area and still have extra space. The poetry display is actually on the back, viewable to people walking up the steps. There’s still extra room on both shelves.

            I had an assignment from another class to design a web page, so I designed a web page centered around the poetry theme. The web address is http://mwilson293.tripod.com

I plan to integrate the web page with the library project. Because the book ordering process is longer than I first thought, it is practical to stretch the monthly theme longer than just one month. Dale Johnson wants me to continue generating the lists for her to order from and the Library Director, James Welbourne, is completely supportive of this project.

            The problems that I’ve encountered are that many books are not in the stacks, although the OPAC says they are.  Another issue that has come up is that many of the authors whose work should be featured are of African descent, but not African-American. Many of the authors are from the Caribbean, Africa, Canada and London. For this reason, the name of the display should be changed. No name has been decided on as of yet.

            The positive outcomes of the display are that people are now browsing the shelves and now that someone has given the area some attention, the librarians on duty are actually going over and straightening up the books.

       B. Censorship and Intellectual Freedom

 

            The list of books that have been banned at various times during our history is also a list of our greatest works of literature. The Bible, Huckleberry Finn, Don Quixote, the Koran, and Tom Sawyer are the books which have been censored the most throughout modern history. The authors of frequently challenged books of today may feel a certain confidence that their work might live on in infamy, thus defeating the very groups that seek to ban their work. Challenges to books almost guarantee a certain notoriety, and thus exposure for the work.

            The American Library Association explains the difference between a challenge and a banning. “A challenge is an attempt to remove or restrict materials based upon the objections of a person or group. A banning is the removal of those materials.” Most of today’s challenges come from members of the community claiming to be Christians. They try to ban books from both school and public libraries, basing their actions on the belief that they are upholding moral values and protecting their children from subversive ideas.

            The reasons for challenging books are based on sexuality, immorality, anti-Christian ideas, and socially objectionable ideas. The lines between these reasons are blurry and overlapping.

            There are some books that neatly fall into the sexuality category. These include books that give biological information about the human body and sexuality. It’s Perfectly Normal by Robie Harris, falls into this category. There is another kind of book featuring sexuality, but focuses on the social acceptance of certain categories of sexuality. An example of this kind of book is Heather Has Two Mommies. Still another type of frequently challenged book that is challenged on the basis of sexuality is the coming of age novels that feature a young person’s foray into sexual experiences, whether voluntarily as depicted in some Judy Blume novels,  or through sexual abuse, like the kind of abuse depicted in I Know Why the Caged Bird Sings.

            When the prolific author Donna Hill read from her work at the New Haven Public Library last spring, she was asked a question concerning another prolific African American writer who writes extremely graphic erotic novels. Ms. Hill obviously disapproved of this person’s work and asked her own question, “Is this what we want to be represented by?” When put that way, most people appreciate Ms. Hill’s point of view. However, as members of a democratic society, we must remember that the way to combat less than wholesome ideas is not to suppress the ideas but to create other ideas. Richared E. Rubin in Foundations of Library and Information Science states, “The generation of good ideas is increased when there is unimpaired freedom to produce them.” P.185

            The list of books thought to promote anti-Christian ideas almost begins with the Harry Potter series. Somehow those who want to sensor believe that books featuring fantasy and magic are an anathema to Christianity. Parents don’t trust their children to know the difference between the make-believe world in books and the world the truth of what they are taught by their parents. It would be censorship on the part of society to try to silence those parents who speak out against these books. The parents have a right to state their opinion and they definitely have a right to censor what their own children read. It is only when they move to restrict what everyone else reads, that they are infringing on  the rights of others. The book that these people claim governs their lives is The Bible, the most censored book in history and full of stories of sexuality, immorality, and even murder. Other often challenged titles with magical or supernatural themes are Bless Me Ultima, The House of the Spirits and Beloved.

            In the discussion of challenged books, there is no topic as heatedly debated as the issue of racism. There are white people who challenge books based on their racism and there are African-Americans who just as vehemently challenge books based on what they perceive as combating racism.

            Many books written by African Americans feature real topics that include rape, incest, drug use, teen parenthood and violence. These books reflect the day to day reality of living in bad neighborhoods in today’s inner cities and life in the post-slavery rural areas. Those (mostly white) people want to shield their children from these harsh realities. They cite reasons like “offensive language” and “graphic sexuality.” They seem to believe that by ignoring the fact that violence and human degradation detailed in these works are the results of the lingering legacy of slavery in this country, they can ignore that ugly part of American history. By ignoring the poverty and hopelessness that exists right now, they can ignore that part of the American present.

            When we read novels like Beloved, The Bluest Eye, The Color Purple, we’re reading about  recently freed slaves, thus learning history. When we read novels like Native Son, we’re reading about the descendants of slaves living in the northern cities still being unable to assimilate into the society.

            In the challenges being brought by African Americans, the most famous example is Huckleberry Finn. The reason is because of the word “nigger” both as a word in itself and as a name in reference to the character “Nigger Jim.” In the story, Nigger Jim is a slave who is “stolen(?)” by Huck and together they raft up the Mississippi River. Jim is depicted as ignorant and subservient to Huck, who is just a boy. If only judged by the time period depicted in the novel, the depiction is accurate. It was against the law to educate a slave, so yes the slave would be ignorant. Slaves were required to act subservient to white people, even a child, so yes the character would be subservient. The word “nigger” was regularly used (and unfortunately still is.)

            The most glaring omission in the entire debate over the novel is the fact that “Nigger Jim” is the moral center of the novel. He shows true friendship to Huck and although Huck is a child, he comes to recognize and value Jim’s humanity and kindness. In the end, Jim is freed.

            The problem with trying to censor reading material is that it seems the would-be censors aren’t reading the entire book and basing their opinion on the merits of the story. It seems that they’re flipping through pages, looking for “bad words” and sexual passages rather than judging the value of the total work. Parents and educators could actually use these so-called offensive words and passages to open up a dialogue with young people, teaching them to discuss topics rather than pretending those topics don’t exist.

C. Biographical Research Project

 

Introduction

            Gerald Salton (also spelled Gerard Salton) was one of the most influential pioneers in the field of Information Retrieval. His work in the 1960’s led the way to everything we use today in retrieving information and documents from databases. Salton’s biggest contribution was developing programs that can answer queries given in natural language, but just as important was his work in indexing and the use of abstracts. It was Salton, who through early experiments, saw the need to divide long documents into partitions that could be identified mathematically by the computer. This removes the need to retrieve an entire book to get an answer to a query contained in one paragraph of that book. Salton’s work was especially useful in the areas of research literature  involving medicine and science and  government documents. Salton is also credited with the early development of hypertext systems we use on the internet today.

            Salton was born Gerhard Anton Sahlmann on March 8, 1927 in Nuremberg Germany. As a boy, he and his family were forced to escape German guards by crossing the border at night. Even after such a traumatic experience, Salton came to the United States, became a citizen, and excelled academically. He earned his bachelors’ and masters degrees in mathematics from Brooklyn College.

            He received his Ph.D. in Applied Mathematics from Harvard in 1958, where he stayed as an instructor then as an associate professor. This is where Salton first developed the collection of computer programs called SMART. In 1965 Salton went to Cornell, where he co-founded the Department of Computer Science. He took the SMART systems with him to Cornell and continued to refine it over 30 years until his death in 1995.

            SMART

            The acronym SMART took on many meanings over the years:

-          System for the Mechanical Analysis and Retrieval of Text

-          System for the Manipulation And Retrieval of Texts

-          Salton’s Magical Automatic Retriever of Text

Before discussing SMART, this paper must acknowledge the Boolean Method of information retrieval, which after much fine-tuning is the most widely used form of retrieving information today. The Boolean method is the result of the work of the mathematician George Boole. The Boolean method allows users to query using keywords along with qualifying words such as “and, “or,” and “not.” This keyword type search is based on the idea that the particular word must show up in the title and in the body of the work. It’s based on exact words. Salton saw that it made no allowances for the different meaning of words and the fact that search engines will not produce documents that have the words phrased another way.

            Keyword searching methods based on exact matching causes a query about Gerald Salton to produce results concerning the Salton Sea and Salton appliances on sale at a Target store. Theoretically, a SMART search system would produce results concerning IR (Information Retrieval) and ASIS (American Society for Information Science) and other results that were truly about Gerald Salton. Informed opinions seem to suggest that the problems with Boolean search engines are the syntax requirements.

The SMART system was designed to process information requests made in natural language. One such system is the “Vector Space Model Search Engine,” which instead of requiring exact word matches, is able to produce documents based on relevance to the idea. It’s important to note that Salton’s work was not a rejection of the Boolean method, but a refiner of the method.

            Since Salton’s early work was funded by the National Science Foundation, most of the documents in his experiments were usually for the government (maybe the military, considering the times,) and therefore never tested in a widespread way. Early commercial online companies rejected Salton’s work as insignificant because of the low volume of documents he used in his trials. There is no evidence that Salton ever courted those companies anyway. His SMART system was “to be used as a lab tool for modeling and evaluation.” (Bourne, p.96)

 

Salton’s Long Career

                        Salton was a prolific writer as well as researcher and instructor. He has written or at least contributed to five IR texts and over one hundred articles. He edited three journals of the ACM (Association for Computing Machinery.) He also belonged to many professional organizations, including the ACM Special Interest Group on Information Retrieval (SIGIR) and was on the Board of Directors for ASIS (American Society for Information Science.)

            Unbelievably, Salton still actually taught classes in addition to everything else he did during his thirty years at Cornell. Salton’s IR Seminar which met weekly, was “renowned throughout the IT community and fondly remembered by his students…” (www.cs.cornell.edu) Salton seemed to be a renaissance man of modern times. He was married with children and grandchildren in addition to being an avid skier and gardener.

He received many awards for his years of research and service to IR. These included:

            The Guggenheim Fellowship 1962

            ASIS Award for Best Information Science Paper 1970

            Best Information Science Book 1975

            The first ACM/SIGIR Award for Outstanding Contributions to Information Retrieval 1983

ASIS Award of Merit 1989

Cornell University’s memoriam of Gerald Salton says that Salton is the person who established IR as a field and boasts that SMART is now the standard upon which modern retrieval systems are based. Today there are many offsprings of the SMART system. These include a news clipping service called Individual (which licensed the technology directly) and DOWQUEST which serves the Dow Jones.

Part I – The Use of CMC in Library Reference Service

 

 

 

Abstract:

            Libraries have embraced computer Mediated Communication (CMC) as the way to win back those patrons who had been lured away by Internet search engines. Forms of CMC include e-mail, instant messaging, chat services, videoconferencing, discussion boards and web pages. While it may be true that the use of CMC has won back some library patrons, there is concern that instead of user satisfaction as the desired goal, the embrace of CMC is based more on libraries’ attempts to present themselves as relevant in today’s digital society. The technology should be a supporting infrastructure in the delivery of patron service, not just technology for its own sake.  Systems can be put into place however, that refocus the emphasis back to user-centered service, by making sure librarians take the time to fully understand patrons’ queries and by adopting safeguards against having patrons’ queries ignored or answered incorrectly.  Reference librarians now have the chance to show that  truly authoritative and professional virtual reference service can be obtained from the library.

 

 

Authorship Statement:

            I certify that this paper and the work it reports are entirely my own. The works of others cited, whether published or unpublished, is acknowledged in accordance with accepted editorial practice.


Introduction

            Using Computer Mediated Communication (CMC) in library reference work is another way of servicing patrons, but in its early stage, CMC is hardly ever used to conduct the reference interview, a necessity in all but the most basic ready-reference questions. Richard Bopp describes the reference interview as “essentially a conversation between a reference staff member and a user, the goal of which is to ascertain the user’s information need and take appropriate action to satisfy that need.” (Bopp p. 47.)

            The use of CMC in answering patron’s reference questions is still relatively new. Literature concerning this specific topic is not all that abundant. There have been several studies however, and almost all of the literature referring specifically to e-mail note the time lapse between patron’s queries and librarians’ responses. Some of the literature stress the absence of non-verbal clues that usually aid communication, and some of the literature describe the types of questions that are better answerable face-to-face rather than in CMC.

 

Sample Studies

            Several studies have been consulted that basically describe the low user satisfaction when using CMC in getting answers to reference questions. Garnsey and Powell, which sent questionnaires to almost 400 libraries in 2000, note the “surprisingly slow response times (in e-mail) considering the nature of the communication.” Kirsti Nilsen, who compared 261 in-person visits to 85 virtual reference transaction (using both e-mail and chat) in an ongoing library visit study, note that “virtual reference results in lower satisfaction than in-person reference. Charlotte Ford’s three-week observation of 12 librarians, noted that librarians communicated less in CMC and provided very little instruction. Eileen Abels, who conducted a three-semester study, using first students then real library clients, notes how “the lack of real time interaction (in e-mail) impacted the reference interview in several (negative) ways.

 

Communication, Time and Responsibility

            Eileen Abels explains the impact of the lack or real time interaction in e-mail reference further, stating, “First, every question or clarification resulted in a delay; several days or even weeks sometimes elapsed before an e-mail reference interview was completed.” Abels has even proposed that “an alternative communication medium should be considered if the question negotiation continues beyond two messages.”

 Joseph Straw states further, “Any effort at clarification in an e-mail encounter requires that a new message be composed and sent. Complex requests run the risk of extending the transaction and delaying the final resolution. “

            Theoretically, a patron who has been seeking other sources while waiting for the librarian’s response could find that his or her information need has changed. The original question is no longer relevant, now there’s a new question. The patron e-mails the information specialist again and the specialist then re-focuses the search. These delays in time between e-mails can subtract from the momentum of the patron/librarian transaction and may result in the patron never actually receiving an answer to his or her query.  Abels has written unequivocally, “In e-mail interviews, non responses to questions are rather common.” As time passes between each message, all except the most diligent information provider will eventually feel a reduced level of responsibility towards answering the question, believing that the patron has found their answer elsewhere.

 

The Effect of Nonverbal Cues

 

            Joseph Straw writes, “In a face-to-face reference interview, the librarian has access to a user’s verbal, non-verbal and visual cues. The user’s tone of voice, patterns of speech, facial expressions and eye contact can give important cues about the course of an encounter.”

            Charlotte E. Ford observed 340 reference interactions divided among face-to-face, chat, and e-mail. She found that “Librarian responses…were quite different across media. The sheer number of librarian utterances was much higher in face-to-face …with more instruction in the use of sources, search terms, and search techniques…librarians were more likely to ask questions of their users in face-to-face interactions…(and) overall, face-to-face reference interactions were found to be “richer” than computer –mediated reference transactions.”

            Kirsti Nilsen further describes the Ford observation, stating, “In e-mail responses, librarians asked few questions or requests for clarification, provided carefully crafted responses, but little instruction. In chat services, librarians were less likely to ask questions than in face to face transactions, and they were less likely to express interest or encouragement or to provide instruction.”

            In an ideal face-to-face librarian/patron exchange, the patron would state their query then watch the information specialist type something into the computer or walk over to the stacks and pull out a reference book. Seeing these actions by the librarian reinforces a sense of librarian-competence for the patron.

            Throughout this process, the patron would have a chance to interject, either adding information or clarifying the question. More importantly, the librarian is telling the patron what sources may be available and is asking questions of the patron in order to pinpoint the exact informational need. It is not just a matter of the questioner asking for an answer, but in the exchange it becomes understood how to get the answer. The negotiation establishes the exact nature of the question, how many sources are needed, the purpose for the question, and the point where the librarian sees that the patron can continue on his or her own.

            Although the above paragraphs depict the face-to-face reference transaction as drastically more beneficial to the patron than a transaction occurring using CMC, Nilsen has found that even in face-to-face reference service, clarifying questions are not asked and the user can still be left unsatisfied. Nilsen states, “Often in face-to-face interviews, the librarian hears the question and begins to type something…after which a piece of paper with a call number scribbled on it is passed to the patron with the comment, ‘Browse in this area.’” Nilsen depicts the parallel of this scenario as it occurs in virtual reference, “the unmonitored referral occurs when URLs are sent with no information attached as to the source of the site or where the needed information can be found in it.”

            Nilsen states that even in chat services, librarians overlook the reference interview and  “report that they feel rushed when doing chat transactions, they worry that the patron is waiting, and feel that they have to send a quick response.”

 

 

 

 

Reference Questions

            The literature defines a ready-reference question as a question of a factual nature “that can be quickly answered consulting only one or two reference tools.” (Bopp p.7)

Given this definition of a ready-reference question, this type of question could be easily answered using CMC. Most difficulties arise when patrons really do not know the difference between a ready-reference question and a research question. Garnsey and Powell define a research question as “More complex and require librarians to check a variety of sources such as books, articles, and encyclopedias in order to find the complete answer.”

            David Carter and Joseph Janes explain, “Users seem to have difficulty in assigning subject categories to their questions and to determine whether they are factual or require sources for assistance.”

            Some reference questions seem as though they are very quickly answerable. Questions like “What is the average daily temperature during summer in New Brunswick?” is easily answerable by using an almanac. Other questions, like, “Can you give me information about hybrid cars?” take a lot more time to first decide on a source and then give an informed answer. Of course, whether the patron is seeking the information for a ninth grade term paper or a graduate thesis or just as a consumer doing research before a purchase makes a difference in the kind of answer given and the types of sources used for reference.

            Knowing the reason for the query helps the information provider to better understand the nature of the question and give a more complete answer. Carter and Janes refer to this idea, saying, “Often, e-mail based questions do not specify sources already consulted, motivation, or reason behind the question, intended uses for the information, and so on. This has a significant impact both on policy and performance.”

 

 

Advantages to using CMC in Reference Transactions

 

            Although the focus of this paper is point out the inadequacies inherent in reference transactions using CMC, it must be noted that there are some possible advantages. The biggest advantage is the fact that there are no geographical boundaries between the user and the librarian. Anyone with a computer can write an e-mail query any time of day or night. What is more, because of regional and state library consortia, most residents of any community now has access to the most extensive reference collections, regardless of what meager reference sources are available at their own neighborhood branch library. These reference sources include not only the physical books and periodicals held by the consortia, but also access to web sites and databases that are only accessible through the library consortia’s subscriptions. Thus, users just by virtue of having a library card, from the most remote areas and the most economically disadvantaged communities are given the same informational opportunities as users from more prosperous or more centrally located towns and cities.

            Another advantage is the fact that in e-mail, the patron can write down their question as soon as it occurs and the act of writing may create clarity. The act of writing forces the information seeker to use precise words that state exactly what he or she is seeking. The patron can read over what they wrote and edit and re-phrase if needed. Of course, the information provider has this same advantage. They can look at the answer he or she has typed and judge if the answer is correct and complete before sending. Of course the flip side of this supposed advantage is the fact that most people do not express themselves as clearly in writing as they would in speaking.

            The advantage for librarians answering reference questions using e-mail is in that the librarians can perform this function during a time slot devoted to that purpose. The librarian can give focused attention to the question at hand. This ability to focus without having a long line of patrons at the reference desk compensates for what communication may be lost using a non face-to-face medium. There is also time for the information provider to make sure the information the patron is seeking is actually located in the items to which the user is referred. (Nilsen 2006).

            Garnsey and Powell go further, stating, “Additionally, e-mail reference would allow librarians who specialized in a particular subject area to answer questions in their specialty, rather than whoever happens to be on the desk.”

 Garnsey and Powell point out another added benefit to e-mail and chat reference, noting, “that it is possible to keep complete and accurate records of questions asked and answers given…and since e-mail questions can be printed out, transcription errors, a common problem in telephone reference, can be avoided.” Joseph Straw goes further, stating, Electronic technology can keep a permanent and reproducible record of the referenced transaction. Libraries can now construct databases of reference questions and responses…(and further) A record of reference transactions can help libraries customize their collection development policies to reflect the sources that are being consulted by remote users.”

From the user’s perspective, it could be stated that a patron could feel more comfortable asking a question that is health-related or a question that could be embarrassing using CMC rather than face-to-face reference service. Some of the literature stress that convenience is the most important reason why patrons use CMC. Nilsen writes, “The convenience of virtual reference is clearly important, and the desire not to get up from the computer to ask at the traditional reference desk has been supported by other findings that show that many of the questions come from on-campus locations and even from patrons who are sitting in the library at workstations.”

 

Improving Reference Service Using CMC

            For most young people, e-mail, instant messaging, and chat are already a way of life. Even older adults who haven’t grown up with digital communication have had to adapt to e-mail, since it e-mail has replaced the paper memo at many places of employment. For students in online classes, e-mail and discussion board manipulation are necessary just to participate in the class. In library reference service, e-mail will rival the telephone as the medium in which information seekers will ask their questions. Therefore, it is imperative that systems are adopted to ensure that every e-mail query is acknowledged and patrons are initially told that someone will research their query. Staff members must be trained to consistently provide timely and courteous service to users so that the CMC transaction feels like real human interaction.

            Abels suggests what she describes as “The Systematic Approach.” She states, …it seemed effective and efficient to begin the process with a request form, as many institutions do for an in-person reference request. The remote reference request form is critical for several reasons. First, the form prompts the client to make a complete statement of the request at the outset. Second, it prompts the client to include information that might otherwise be omitted, such as language requirements or type of materials sought.” Abels, later in the article, suggests other kinds of information on the proposed form such as deadline, time period, and geographic area.

            While Abels concentrates on ways to make the patron’s question clearer, Joseph Janes emphasizes giving the transaction a more personable feel. Janes advocates the use of names in e-mail reference transactions, to make the transaction more human centered and feel more like face-to-face. He discusses a recent student’s findings concerning e-mail reference users’ perceived satisfaction with the service. Janes states, “In general, users were more satisfied and had more positive reactions to their experiences when names were involved – when librarians used their own names and referred to users by name.” He further argues, “Would you trust or even use an accountant, attorney, doctor or designer you didn’t know by name?”

            Another idea proposed for keeping a log of e-mail transactions along with findable answers and sources is to keep a blog at the reference desk. Emily Barton and Arlene Wesmantel came up with this idea at the University of Michigan Library. The reference desk had previously been using a notebook to share a list of questions received and the sources consulted in answering the questions. The staff had become frustrated having to flip through the pages of the notebook, searching for sources used in earlier similar e-mails. Wesmantel suggested using a blog because a blog is searchable, either by date, category or phrase. Only the reference staff has access to write on the blog, but the entire library staff has access to view it.

 

            Conclusion

            CMC as a medium in reference work is very limited in answering patron’s queries. The literature suggests that because the information specialist is more likely to bypass the reference interview in this medium, the patron is never given the chance to explain the exact nature of their information need. Although the face-to-face librarian/patron exchange is optimal for establishing a successful reference interview, the librarian should make every effort to clarify the patron’s questions using CMC. Knowing the reason for the question helps the librarian to tailor the sources and level of information to the patron’s need.

 The time delay between asking, qualifying, and answering queries especially in e-mail makes it a less desirable way to communicate. Even in chat, users and information specialists are unable to hear each other’s voices, see each other’s facial expressions, or judge each other’s tones. The librarian is at a disadvantage, being unable to judge whether or not the patron understands the answer, needs more information, or is satisfied with the service. However, as Nilsen has pointed out, even in face-to-face reference, if the librarian merely hands the patron a call number, “the in-library patron wanders off to find the suggested section of the library, does not find anything helpful and leaves disappointed.” This statement emphasizes that although reference through CMC has inherent difficulties, all reference service whether virtual or in-person, must be administered with thoroughness and professionalism.

            Ready-reference questions may be easier to navigate using CMC, but because many patrons don’t know the difference between a factual question and a research question. Research questions are commonly sent using CMC and patrons are apt to end up as dissatisfied users, expecting a speedy reply.

            In spite of the drawbacks to CMC in reference work, there are many advantages, including giving access to consortia library holdings without geographical boundaries, prompting patrons to write out their question so there is a permanent transcript, the convenience patrons enjoy in not having to leave their homes or even their computer work station in the library, and creating anonymity for patrons who might be embarrassed asking some questions in person.

            Ironically, the very techniques that have been initiated to make CMC more efficient in reference practice such as online request forms and complete and searchable records of previously asked questions and possible sources are the very technologies that could make the professional reference librarian superfluous. Indeed, some theorists have noted that ever since the inception of the online public access catalog (OPAC), end users have been searching for bibliographic information using the skills needed for multifaceted searching which were once only held by librarians.

            “These days, patrons who once would have called a librarian to check on a citation or look up an item in the library catalog are probably using the library’s web page to get their answers. (Coffman 2004.)

            It is also argued that today’s reference seeker, proficiently trained in the use of searching dot-coms, actually prefers the type of independent searching now made available by electronic library service. M.C. Wilson writes, “The relationship between the librarian and the user was hierarchical…in which reference librarians were accorded expertise regarding information resources and access.”

            Since the development of the OPAC, accessible from user’s homes, has made searching for bibliographic information a user process rather than a librarian process, it is simply a natural progression that searching for factual information would become a user process as well. Thus, many library web pages have been enhanced with the software needed to create virtual reference services such as “ask a librarian” and “24/7 reference.”

            All of the literature repeatedly stress that the key to the success of electronic reference is to keep the user at the center of the service. Each writer focuses on the importance of the reference interview as the factor which showcases superior library reference service over the dot.coms.

            Joseph Straw states, “Despite changes in communication technology, the reference interview will remain at the heart of the reference transaction. The ability to understand, question, and communicate remains as important in the electronic world as in face-to-face encounters.”

            Terence Huwe strongly believes in the superiority of library reference service over commercial web sites and bases this belief on the effective use of the reference interview. He states, “At the core of the interchange lies a simple, but powerful moment in the interview. Usually it involves the reference provider saying, in a casual sort of way, “What were you really hoping to find?” …(Then) the service provider has an opportunity to demonstrate library skill, which depends upon a comprehensive, thoughtful approach to using all media, both new and historical, and both digital and print.”

            This comprehensive approach as described above should be considered when discussing the necessity of professional librarianship in the virtual environment. Even using CMC, reference work should be conducted with the user’s need always at the forefront of the service. The technological enhancements are the means to this end.

Part II - Development of a Basic Reference Collection

            This proposed reference collection is such that would be found in an African American Art Museum (not a history museum.) The focus is on visual still art including paintings, sculptures, quilts, and dolls. The proposed collection is limited to African American Artists of the United States only, beginning from the 1600’s until the present time. The collection is to be contained in a small library or reading room inside of an African American Art Museum which would be frequented by art historians, art students, art instructors, and museum patrons. The space should be large enough to include shelves for browsing, comfortable chairs for reading, two computer workstations to access web or database sources and two tables for note taking. The criteria used for selecting materials are based on articles from Black Issues Book Review, which is a literature magazine but has a section devoted to fine arts in each issue. Some selections are recommended from an article in Library Journal: “The Visual Soul of Black Art” by Edward K. Owusu-Ansah. 130.18 (Nov. 1, 2005): p.49 (3)
