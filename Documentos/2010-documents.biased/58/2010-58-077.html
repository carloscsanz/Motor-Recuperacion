Tips for choosing the best car seat

Never use a hand-me-down or used car seat. Standards change security and technology continues to improve. Although it is tempting to save a little money, your child's safety not worth the risk. Accept these clothes hand-me-down, and buying the new car seat.

Find the right seat at the age of your child and weight. There are several types of car seats on the market. Car seats for infants or "carriers" often come with a base removable. The base is secure in the car with seat belts and the carrier part is out so you can take the baby in the house or store. You can also buy child seats that clip right onto your stroller. Baby seats for children over 20 pounds and a year longer large and remains attached to the car all the time. Booster seats can be used by children who are 4'9 "and taller.

If you travel often, make sure the car seat is FAA approved for air travel. Your child is much more secure in his seat car when traveling on the plane - if you experience turbulence, it will be safely buckled in.

Installing the seat correctly. Infants up to 20 lbs, or less than one year of age should always ride facing the rear. Be sure to carefully follow the instructions for installation - many parents feel they have their car seat correctly installed, but have not followed all the directions. Check the website of the American Academy of Pediatrics for installation instructions and other tips: http://www.aap.org/family/carseatguide.htm

Check to see if your car seat has been recalled. You can call the Auto Safety Hotline at 888/DASH-2-DOT (888/327-4236) or visit the National Highway Traffic Safety Administration (NHTSA) in www-odi.nhtsa.dot.gov/cars/problems/recalls / childseat.cfm.

Consider buying more than one seat self for your child. Properly install a child seat can take time. If you have more than one car or the child will often travel with grandparents or caregivers, buying seats on each car. This will save time and ensure that every seat is installed correctly.

Replace car seats that were involved in a car accident. A moderate collision can compromise security your child seat. NHTSA considers a crash minor if all conditions are met:

a. The vehicle could be driven from the accident.

b. The door nearest the safety seat to the car was not damaged.

c. Nobody in the vehicle was wounded.

d. Air bags do not turn off.

e. You can not see the damage to car safety seat.

Always install child seats in the back seat, especially if your car is equipped with air bags. A cushion of air is lethal to a small child, even if they are safe seat. Some vehicles, like trucks that have Only One row of seats, are equipped an on / off switch for passenger air bags. In this case, disable the airbag.
