The Day The Waves Came

  
ANONGNAT SARTPISUT
  
NATCHA YUTTAWORAWIT
talk to Phuket Governor Udomsak Uswarangkura about the lessons learned from the December 26, 2004 tsunami, and hear his story of what happened in the disaster’s aftermath.

It was a Sunday Phuket Governor Udomsak Uswarangkura will never forget. He first heard that something had happened when a friend called, saying there had been flooding in Karon.

He went to the Provincial Hall to await a call confirming a trip to Koh Racha in Khao Lak by HRH Princess Ubol Ratana Rajakanya and, meanwhile, rang the police to check the flooding report.

“My friend at Karon had told me it had been tidal waves, a first wave and then a second coming across the road,” Gov Udomsak told the Gazette. “So I contacted the police, asking them to tell me what the situation was.”

The news was not good. The Governor took a helicopter to check damage at Chalong, Patong, Kamala and Bang Tao: “I saw many broken boats, and umbrellas and chairs floating in the sea. At Patong damaged boats and cars were strewn in the street. I could see the damage was severe.

“After my bird’s eye view I called the Meteorological Office, and they told me there had been a tsunami. I asked, ‘Are you sure?’, and they said it had happened after an earthquake measuring 9.2 on the Richter Scale. I knew we had an extremely serious situation on our hands.”

The Governor examined a contingency plan that had been drawn up for use in the event of a natural disaster, but quickly decided it was unworkable in these circumstances. It was also not up to date.

“I then co-ordinated five units that could work together – doctors, the Office of Disaster Prevention and Mitigation (ODPM), district officers, local administration organizations and the police,” he said.

“I ordered the Provincial Operations Center (POC) at Phuket Provincial Hall to be opened, and had [extra] phones installed. I got the Phuket Provincial Administration Office to ask all groups involved to work out of the POC. We updated Bangkok on the situation, reporting [what numbers we had of] the dead and injured.”


‘Everything about our response to the tsunami, both good and bad, will be written in the history books’
         Governor Udomsak Uswarangkura


Prime Minister Thaksin Shinawatra arrived in the evening from Khon Kaen Province.

“I realized that many foreigners wanted us to repatriate them,” the Governor said. “The PM ordered Thai Airways to help. He said that as many people as possible [who wanted to go] should be taken to Don Muang International Airport or Tham-masat University in Bangkok. We needed to help those injured, many of whom had lost all their money, possessions and documents.”

The 38 foreign ambassadors accredited to Thailand began arriving in Phuket, and an International Assistance Unit (IAT) was set up at the Provincial Administration Organization (OrBorJor) building.

“I used to be Director-General of the Civil Defense Division of the Department of Local Administration in Bangkok, and one of my responsibilities was disaster management,” Gov Udomsak said. “I used this experience, and the help of some of my [former] colleagues who also knew how to cope with such an event.”

It became clear from early on that communications systems would have a problem coping in the aftermath. Mobile phone networks rapidly became overloaded, and all but useless.

“I had to use amateur radio links to communicate with other officials,” he said. “[For example], the first night, while we were awaiting the PM’s arrival at Chai football field, we received requests by radio to pick up people from the Phi Phi Islands.”

“By the end of the first day, we had organized emergency equipment to help clear the beach with the help of soldiers. They worked with OrBorTor and municipality staff to begin clearing away wreckage, starting in the early morning of the second day. The task was urgent.”

Doctors continued to help the injured, and also co-operated with the police to identify the bodies of victims whenever that was possible.

After the PM’s decision on the first night to evacuate people to Bangkok, the Governor went to Phuket International Airport on Day Two.

“I asked how many airlines could fly people to Bangkok, because I saw there were around 500 people waiting in the multi-purpose meeting hall at the airport. The airport director said he needed to give priority to people who already had tickets, but I said ‘No, that’s not what PM Thaksin ordered’.

“I phoned Bangkok, saying we needed more aircraft, and that we had to talk to the low-cost airlines so we could help people. I also said we would have to apologize to passengers in other areas of the country [if their flights were canceled] because planes were needed to evacuate people from Phuket.”

On the second night the Governor went to Patong with officials from the Ministry of the Interior.

“It was very calm and quiet, with nobody around,” he said. “With no electricity, there was complete darkness. It was around midnight when I took them to Patong. We had a moment’s silence for everyone [who had died].

He then went to the airport, where he stayed until midnight. The airlines co-operated well with the authorities, he said, both in Phuket and in Bangkok: “On December 27 and 28, we repatriated about 4,000 people. We tried to move as many people as possible.”

So what lessons were learned during the first few days following the disaster?

“We needed better public relations,” Gov Udomsak said. “Some people did not understand what we were doing, but the priority had to be foreign tourists: they are Phuket’s customers, so we helped them first.

“Of course, we helped Thais, too, but we still received complaints that we were ignoring them. It was a crucial time, and everyone needed help.”

Since the tsunami, new procedures have been put in place, based on the experiences of the many different groups of officials involved in managing the aftermath of the disaster.

“I have created an internal task force for each department,” he said. “Each unit has to be able to work together with all the others – this is the key to success. We now also have an evacuation plan [for moving large numbers of people] for Phuket airport.”

On March 28 this year, there was a major alert following another big undersea earthquake centered off southern Sumatra – similar to the seismic event that gave birth to the December 26 tsunami.

Thankfully, there was no repeat of last year’s catastrophe, but the March scare did highlight shortcomings in the new disaster response system. Again, the focus was on communications.

“We had to use mobile cell masts and radios,” Gov Udomsak said.

“After that [March 28 alert] I supported the amateur radio association in setting up a mobile unit to help in times of trouble. It gives us a communications link between the tambon administration organizations (OrBorTor), the OrBorJor and the municipalities.”

Redundancy – or duplication of systems – is a tactic well known in many industries, and the Governor believes it is just as important in dealing with disasters.

“There may even be duplication” in aid efforts, he said, “with some people getting more than enough help because so many organizations are working to help them – Civil Defense and student volunteers, the Thai Red Cross ... But I don’t mind. People get help, and that is what matters.”

One thing he does stress, however, is that management of aid has to be seen to be fair. “We tried to make the question of disaster-relief donations completely clear and transparent,” he said.

“This was a major event in history, and I had to account for everything. I asked my vice-governors who were responsible for each specific task to ensure there was proper documentation [on how the money was spent].

“Everything about our response to the tsunami, both good and bad, will be written in the history books,” he added. “I don’t want my name or reputation to be bad in this case. I did everything I could to make sure it was good.”

How the new tsunami warning system works


Warning towers at Saphan Hin and Patong


ANDY JOHNSTONE talks to the Deputy Director of the National Disaster Warning Center about the new system.

That the tsunami struck Thailand with no warning having been issued is a fact that remains uncomfortable for many, not just for those who lost relatives in the disaster.

The frustration still felt was expressed by families of 91 Britons who died in the tsunami, and whose inquests were held in London between December 5 and 8. The relatives are a world away from the beach resorts of the Indian Ocean, and although Phuket is back, their loved ones never can be.

Sally Pook reported in the Daily Telegraph on December 6 that Liz Jones, whose daughter, Charlotte, 24, died on Koh Racha Yai, said, “If my daughter had had five minutes, she would have been alive today.”

Speaking at the mass inquest, expert witness Dr Tim Henstock of the University of Southampton’s School of Earth and Ocean Science was reported by the BBC as saying, “The people who knew the earthquake had happened were not in a position to do anything in terms of warning on the ground.”

He continued, “An early warning system does not help unless you have infrastructure and mechanisms, and everyone knows what is supposed to be done if a warning comes in.”

Phuket, indeed Thailand, now has a warning system, controlled from the National Disaster Warning Center (NDWC) in Nonthaburi, and the infrastructure and mechanisms are in place.

The well-publicized drill conducted in Patong on December 16, which saw around 200 people take part, was declared a success despite, ironically, the rain.

However, more telling were the events of just two days earlier, when a “silent test” – these are conducted twice a day – was anything but. For reasons unknown, the alarms in all six provinces with tsunami warning towers sounded at noon.

Although reports are vague and precise figures difficult to obtain, it appears that many people on the beaches or otherwise close to the shore did exactly what they should have done under the circumstances: they ran for their lives.

The system does appear to be working – even though the network of 62 warning towers has yet to be completed.

Air Chief Marshal Anuphan Sanitwong na Ayutthaya, Deputy Director of the NDWC, gave an insight to the system.

He explained first that the threat to Phuket and the other five provinces covered by the system stems from the Sunda Trench Fault (STF), which marks the convergent boundary of principally the Sunda and Burma Plates, with the India and Australian Plates.

The subduction of the India plate beneath the Burma micro-plate west of Sumatra, some 700 kilometers southwest of Phuket, caused the earthquake at 7:58:53 am local time that triggered the December 26 tsunami.

“The trench – its closest point it is approximately 450km due west of Phuket – runs more-or-less north to south from just north of the Andaman and Nico-bar Islands running southwest of Sumatra,” ACM Anuphan said, adding, “[because of] seismic activity in this area, these plates are the focus of the NDWC’s attention.”

A tsunami, if generated by submarine seismic activity along the STF, would take around two hours to reach Phuket.

Information on seismic activity is gathered – and shared – by the Thai government and neighboring countries, particularly Malaysia and Indonesia. Thailand also monitors information from Germany, the UK, Japan and the US.

“The NDWC, which has sole and complete responsibility to issue warnings in Thailand – and of course is manned around the clock – will analyze data collected, with particular regard to the magnitude of an earthquake, its depth and the type of seismic movement.”

ACM Anuphan said that an earthquake occurring 100 or more kilometers beneath the seabed is unlikely to cause an tsunami. “The closer to the surface, then the more likely a tsunami is,” he said.

The United States Geological Survey website says that the December 26 quake occurred approximately 30km below the seabed.

ACM Anuphan said that the movement of the plates – strike-slip or thrust-fault – is also checked. Major earthquakes tend to be thrust-fault earthquakes, which are more likely to cause a tsunami, such as that of December 26, which is referred to as a “megathrust” event.

He warned that it is impossible to determine whether an earthquake was strike-slip or thrust-fault in less than three hours. “This is more than the length of time it would take a tsunami to reach the areas of Thailand deemed at risk from the STF,” he cautioned.

“Additionally, and although we can make an accurate and almost immediate determination of the magnitude of a quake, the precise location takes 20 to 25 minutes to determine.”

ACM Anuphan said that an earthquake of magnitude (M) 3 to 4.9 cannot generate a tsunami, and although unlikely, a M5.0 to M5.9 earthquake could. “The NDWC would issue an advisory to the Prime Minister and other Cabinet members, as well as governors [of the six tsunami-affected provinces] if an event of this magnitude occurred.

“A tsunami would be unlikely, yes, but we would issue the advice [to them] to prevent the spread of rumors.

“From M6.0 to M6.9 there is a low chance of a tsunami and the same procedures will be followed, although we will monitor the situation much more carefully.

“The chance of a tsunami increases with M7.0 to M7.9 earthquakes, and the NDWC enters the ‘alert and watching’ stage, with careful analysis of sea levels from tidal gauges in Thailand and the region, and water-pressure data from various organizations such as the Intergovernmental Oceanographic Commission and Pacific Tsunami Warning Center,” he said.

“The tidal gauges in the region are too close to the shore to give adequate warning, but Thailand is soon to start deploying deep-sea buoys in her [maritime] economic zone between Anda-man coastal regions and the Andaman and Nicobar Islands. These will be around 200km apart.” [Thailand’s maritime exclusive economic zone is 200 nautical miles, or 370km.]

Currently, the network of DART (Deep-ocean Assessment and Reporting of Tsunamis) stations established in 1995 by the US National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration (NOAA) is limited to six, all in the Pacific.

Each DART station consists of a seabed pressure-recording (BPR) unit that detects pressure changes caused by tsunamis and a surface buoy which receives information transmitted from the BPR via an acoustic link. The buoy transmits data to a geostationary satellite, which retransmits the data to ground stations. An additional 32 DART stations are to be installed by mid-2007.

Satellite-based radar, too, is employed to monitor wave height, as are GPS-equipped inertia-system buoys.

“The movement of an inertia-system buoy in a tsunami is very different to the movement it would experience if thrown by waves or even a swell,” ACM Anuphan said.

“This information is also collected, monitored and used in the complex computer modeling required to determine if a tsunami is approaching.”

The alert and watching stage – M7.0 and above – will be when evacuation warnings to the public will be given.

The information will be disseminated by SMS to Governors, Vice-Governors, heads of Tam-bon Administration Organizations (OrBorTor), the police, hospitals and rescue personnel, he said. It will also be relayed automatically by fax and email.

The public will be warned through NDWC-controlled interruption of television and radio broadcasts, with messages giving the estimated time of an impending tsunami will strike the coast, and the sounding of the warning tower sirens.

The towers, 18 spread around the major beaches of Phuket and smaller neighboring islands, but not as yet at Nai Harn Beach, will broadcast simple messages in Thai, English, German, Japanese and Chinese. Messages will be broadcast at 127dB, similar to the volume of a jet airliner at 100 meters, and should be audible up to 1.5km away.

ACM Anuphan said that information will be sent via an INMARSAT satellite, with hardened backup landlines as a redundancy measure should INMAR-SAT fail to respond.

“INMARSAT provides telephony and data services to users worldwide, via digital radios called ‘terminals’. An INMAR-SAT terminal contacts the satellite and communicates to a ground station through the satellite.”

The system provides reliable communications services to a range of governments and aid agencies in remote regions, where there is no reliable terrestrial network, or as a ‘redundant’ method.

“If that fails too, then this is the only situation in which humans are involved in activating the towers manually. Manual faxes and SMS would then be sent to chief police officers and governors of the provinces.”

TIMINGS

“It will take a maximum of two minutes to send the signal to the towers and TV and radio stations,” said ACM Anuphan.

“I heard that one governor does not want to cede [to the NDWC] control of the warnings in his province. He will be spoken to.”

The Air Chief Marshal said that reconfirmation details would be sent as and when a clearer picture emerged, adding, “…but this should take place no later than five minutes for email and SMS and 10 minutes for fax after the initial alert is given. My best time for sending the reconfirmation alerts is 90 seconds.”

He stressed that Thailand’s deep-sea buoys would not be a first line of defense – warnings will have been given of a potential or confirmed tsunami before it reaches them – but they would be a tool to confirm or reconfirm that one is approaching.

“The information they relay will give us 15 minutes’ warning.”

“The NDWC monitors, detects, analyzes and warns,” ACM Anuphan said.

“From that point on it is up to the local authorities to implement evacuation plans.”

Life can be good again


The last photograph Franky took of his parents, on Loy Krathong night, 2004, with (right) Swedish neighbor and friend Torbjoern ‘Tobbe’ Siegfrid-sson, who also died in the disaster, as did his wife Maude.

FRANKY GUN lost so much a year ago. But the Gazette man in Khao Lak is a survivor

When I was asked to write about the Khao Lak area in the year after the tsunami, I had mixed feelings. I was directly affected by the disaster, losing my beloved parents, great friends and neighbors, my pets, house, car and almost everything else I owned. I too almost drowned. It has not been pleasant digging around my post-tsunami memories, especially those of the months of January to March. Yet there were good times too, such as the birth of my son Martin in June.

This personal account is meant to show the emotional and psychological recovery a tsunami survivor experiences: it is not an exercise in self-pity, but a possible aid to understanding the scope of the tragedy – and to prove that emotional recovery is quite possible. I hope it will help readers who have lost loved ones to gain emotional strength by realizing that they are not alone.

The end of December, 2004

The shock sits deep. I am slowly realizing that Mom and Dad are not only gone for ever, but that their bodies may not even be found. I don’t feel like a survivor, and am not happy about surviving.

My wife “Lad”, who is four months pregnant, and her best friend “Porn” also survived – they were attending classes at the Rajhabat University in Phuket that Sunday. It’s bizarre, but I can’t be even grateful for that right now, as there is so much death, destruction and grieving around me. Out of the 15 people closest to our house, eight are dead, including Mom and Dad. The death toll in Khao Lak alone has reached 3,000 and keeps rising.

Whenever we drive through Khao Lak, on the way to the temples and hospitals to search for my parents, I have to weep. I’m crying a lot these days, even in my sleep. Our house in Bang Niang is badly damaged, yet most walls are standing. All the other buildings around us have been flattened. It’s like a war zone. I am too sad and tired to pick up the bits and pieces of our possessions that have been scattered all round the house. The feeling is of cold emptiness; I don’t want anything any more.

Suddenly we find my favorite dog, “Dickie”, wandering through the rubble. I feel happiness for the first time since the 26th, hugging him and crying like a baby.

We buy a mobile phone in Takuapa so we can be reached. Friends and relatives contact me after receiving my phone call for help from the emergency line at Takuapa Hospital, or after seeing TV interviews with me on Spiegel and Fox; they called the TV stations to asking for my contact details.

An outbreak of water-borne diseases is feared, and I decide to stop the search for my parents’ remains as it will be safer for Lad and the unborn child to move to Phuket. We find shelter at the house of a good friend, Stefan, after spending one night at a hotel. Then we lose “Dickie” because the management ask us to put him on a leash outside. In the morning he is gone. I’m devastated. Continued searches yield no results.

January 2005

It helps being around friends, and it is great to see how many people truly care about us. Stefan and his wife Nud say we can stay as long as we like, but we decide to get back on our own feet ASAP. Lad finds a house in Kathu and we decide to rent it until we figure out what to do next.

I try to keep busy and get my paperwork back in order, as every single document from passport to work permit, company papers to driving licenses, bankbooks, credit cards and land papers are all gone.


The farewell at his parents’ home in Germany.


Many tiring hours of driving between Khao Lak and Phuket pay off – on a trip back to Phuket we find Dickie at the Thalang intersection. He was on his way home to Khao Lak and managed to walk 25 kilometers in the right direction unharmed, despite the heavy traffic. We can’t believe our luck, and cry and laugh at the same time. Dickie bounces round in the car like a kangaroo gone mad.

The Gazette asks me to describe my experiences of December 26 for the newspaper. The feature proves to be a great help for readers who lost family and friends in Khao Lak, as they get a detailed description of the brute force of a tsunami; many now realize that the chances of survival, especially for children and older people, were next to none.

Many also find it is now time to face the fact that their parents, brothers, sisters and children who have not been found alive will likely never return. I receive many readers’ letters thanking me and the newspaper, and wishing me all the best.

The process of writing about it has helped a lot in the emotional recovery process. Yet I still find it hard to get a good night’s sleep; I have flashbacks and dream about the tsunami.

In some dreams I meet my parents, and it is as if the disaster had never happened. But then I ask them, “How can you be here? That’s impossible…” And I wake up, angry at myself for not being able to talk to them properly.

I am not in good shape, to say the least. My best friend Manfred, from Germany, decides to visit us and cheer us up around my birthday. We stay at a resort in Krabi for about 10 days, trying to ease the pain of post-tsunami trauma.

February 2005

We get the reconstruction permit for our house in Bang Niang. My friend Norbert has provided some space for me in his property office. Via phone, email and with Manfred’s help I manage to sort out most of the legal issues in Germany regarding my missing parents.

As the only son it is my responsibility to get things done, including canceling the rental contract for their flat and clearing the place out. This requires a trip to Germany. I’m horrified at the thought of stepping into my parents’ home in Witten for the first time since losing them.


Closure: Franky and Lad commit his parents’ ashes to the deep.

All my childhood I lived there. I am worried that my memories, the smells, colors, photos and other things will cause an emotional breakdown. I hope for the best and book the ticket anyway. I do not feel good about this at all.

My kids Stella and Marco back in Germany, who will be out of school soon, promise to lend me emotional support. Lad wants to come too, but I feel it would cause her unnecessary stress – which would not be good for our unborn baby. I decide to fly alone.

March 2005

On arrival at Duesseldorf Airport in Germany I’m picked up by my ex-wife, who drives me to my uncle’s house, where I will stay for the next three weeks. The next day, I buy a nice pot of flowers for Mom and a large candle to take home, and I also take a pack of the Thai cigarettes my Dad loved to smoke so much.

I decide to go in alone. The well-known scent, the sound of the clock in the kitchen… thousands of memories flash past in a second. I manage to hold back the tears. I sit in the kitchen, my parents’ favorite room. I place a photo of them on the table, beside the flowers, light the candle and a cigarette for Dad, and say hello. Tears roll down my cheeks as I talk to them. It is a sad monologue, yet I feel their presence much stronger here. When I am more composed, I walk around the flat, trying to concentrate on what needs to be done.

The Bundeskriminalamt (BKA, the Federal Criminal Police) has been here, taking fingerprints and hair samples for DNA analysis. They made quite a mess.

I have been given a strict deadline from the landlord for clearing the flat, including complete removal of the wallpaper and carpets. I am back in Germany, “Strict Regulations and No Exceptions Land”.

During the following weeks, my children, former wife, old classmates and friends help me to get everything done in time. Calling Lad also helps to keep me focused. The flowers that I bought start to die, despite being watered, and it feels as if the presence of my parents has now gone. I feel very sad.

When the flat is besenrein – “broom clean” – it is just a cold empty shell. Handing over the keys to the landlord is easy.


Franky, Lad and Martin, who was born six months after the tsunami.

At the end of March I receive two phone calls from the BKA, telling me that my Mom and Dad have been identified, using dental records. I am relieved and happy that now we can hold a proper burial ceremony. Many tears are shed during my three weeks in Germany, but I also have great times with my friends and my kids. When I board the plane back home to Thailand, it feels as if a great weight has been lifted from my shoulders.

April to November 2005

These seven months are best described as the “healing phase”. After the funeral at Wat Phra Thong in Phuket in early April, we organize a ceremony off Khao Lak to scatter my parents’ ashes. It is a mixed ceremony, with both Thai Buddhist and Christian elements.

The sun is shining and the sea is as smooth as a mirror. My parents’ bones and ashes are white like snow; they shine like stars while sinking to the ocean floor. As I look at the bright colors of flower petals floating on the surface, I feel at peace for the first time since December 26.

I know my parents are in a better place now, and that they wouldn’t want us to grieve any longer. I gain much emotional strength that day, and am happy that so many friends attend the ceremony at Cape Tab Lamru. We will be able to see the cape from our new home at Saiyoi Forest, in the hills of southern Khao Lak.

From that day on everything improves quickly; business is up, and with the financial help of friends and family, we are able to rebuild much of what was lost. The emotional scars remain, but they will fade in time.

I decide that other relatives of victims may be more in need of help than me, and I write about how to deal with loss and how to grieve in tsunami-related Khao Lak Internet forums. This work proves very rewarding.

The highlight of the healing phase is the birth of our son Martin on June 16. In the first few minutes after he is born, he looks a bit like my Dad; then I recognize some of my Mom’s facial expressions.

But then everyone says, “Looks like you, Franky. Looks like you!”

It is at this point that I realize there are universal forces at work, and that nothing really ever ends or dies. Life feels wonderful again.

Emotionally, I am now a much stronger and more stable person. I am grateful for having survived, and also for having so many friends out there – more than I ever expected. My thanks to all of you for being there when I needed you the most.


‘They actually thought that maybe everything in the world had gone, that they were the last survivors’

Olle and Jakob are taken off Temptation by dinghy after their rescue.


ALASDAIR FORBES talks to a couple who rescued two Swedes who had spent more than 26 hours in the sea

Salee Goodman was in low spirits as she and husband Tam upped anchor early in the morning of December 26, 2004, and sailed out of Nai Harn Bay, heading north for the waters of Burma.

It was just about a year since her daughter, her son-in-law and their three children had all died in a domestic accident, poisoned by carbon monoxide.

What she and Tam saw from their yacht, Temptation, would only add to Salee’s gloom.

They were just north of the Sarasin Bridge when the tsunami swept past them. “We felt a slight swell,” Tam explains, “and then we saw the wave break along the coast. We thought, ‘Wow, that was a big wave’, but it wasn’t exceptional, because the shoreline there is straight, so it wasn’t accentuated.

“It was only 20 minutes after that that we got calls saying, ‘Are you alive? A tidal wave has hit Phuket.’ We decided there was no point in turning around, so we kept heading north.”

It was only when they got close to Tab Lamu, where they planned to drop anchor for the night, that the full horror was revealed.

“We started seeing all the debris, and suitcases and pillows, bits of houses and trees, and shortly afterwards we started to see bodies,” says Tam.

Salee adds, “We saw a lot of people, 50 or more. They were all dead. Some spots we couldn’t go. We had to stop and push them away before we could go on.” She confesses that, with her sadness over her lost family and then this, she was close to a breakdown.

They pushed on and anchored in the fading light of day. Night fell and the whole coast was pitch black. “There was not a single light,” says Salee.

At first light they headed north again. They passed Nang Tong Beach in Khao Lak. “It was devastated,” says Tam. “It was wiped out.”

“There was no life – no boats, no helicopters,” says Salee.

As the day brightened, however, the signs were there: “Off Khao Lak we saw all these bodies in the water, but the helicopters were over the land,” Salee says.

It was off Koh Phra Tong that they finally came across life on the water – faint voices calling for help.

“When we finally located the direction of their calls for help and we changed our course and went toward them, the current was very strong and as we got closer, we had to maneuver around them.”

The two survivors were a father and son, Olle and Jakob Fagring from Sweden. They had been clinging to debris for 26 hours.

Tam says, “When we first brought them onto the boat, they told us that, since the time they had capsized they had never seen the sight or sound of any human life.

“There had never been an airplane, or a helicopter or a boat of any kind for 26 hours. They actually thought that maybe everything in the world had gone, that they were the last survivors.”


Tam and Salee Goodman.

The two had been staying at a quiet resort on Koh Kor Khao, just north of Baan Nam Khem, and were out on a long-tail boat with Olle’s wife Karen, the couple’s older son, Per, and Per’s girlfriend.

The wave hit and Olle and Jakob were flung overboard. The rest stayed with the boat and rapidly drifted out of sight.

“A current took them further out to sea. When they got tired they stopped swimming and just clung to wreckage,” says Tam. “When we picked them out of the water, they were completely sunburned from the chest up, but their feet and legs were already hypothermic – already blue.”

“I don’t think they would have lived through the next night,” Salee remarks. “The first thing we did was to give them clothes and water, and some candy bars. We’d just filled up the fridge, so we said, ‘Which would you like?’ They took them all. Every bite they took, they cried. I told them we were so happy that we could help them.”

“They were on an emotional roller-coaster,” says Tam. “They were so happy to be rescued yet they were also horrified at the thought they had lost their family.”

Salee and Tam eventually managed to find a small port near Kuraburi where they could drop the two, wearing borrowed clothing and carrying US$200 the Goodmans gave them, along with a letter Salee wrote for them in Thai, describing what had happened and who they were, and asking anyone who read it to help them.

“They said later that letter was like a passport,” says Salee. “Everything they asked for was done, and that night they discovered the rest of the family had been rescued and were back at the resort, which had not been damaged too badly.

Why there were not more survivors in the water is a mystery to Tam and Salee. “We kept looking and hoping. It seems like there should have been a lot more.”

But just rescuing the two Swedes was a huge boost for Salee. “When I saw those two guys I changed. My spirits just soared. Just two lives. After all the dead bodies.”

Twelve months in Khao Lak
By FRANKY GUN

Khao Lak was battered and bruised by the tsunami a year ago. But in spite of all the anti-Khao Lak and pro-Phuket politics, next to nothing in government aid and slow bank loans, the spirit of the Khao Lak people remains unbroken. It survived and is strong again today because of its people; they had to fight against all odds, but still refused to buckle under the pressure. Khao Lak is a big family, and no one can take that away from us. Here’s a diary of the past year in the battling town I now call my home.

End of December 2004: The local people, along with dive-shop staff, are the heroes of the moment. Immediate rescue efforts are saving hundreds of lives. The roads are being cleared as much as bare hands can manage, enabling the injured to be taken to Takuapa and Thai Muang.

Temporary shelters and makeshift first aid stations are everywhere. Volunteers and other rescuers work day and night. There is a minor police presence; nobody really knows what is going on.

There are constant rumors about more tsunamis on the way. Many people are staying in the mountains without food or water because they fear another hit. Looters stoke the panic by spreading gossip that more waves are coming.

While local people engage in first aid and collect the dead, outsiders are moving in with profit in mind, searching the clothing of corpses along the road for valuables.

Finally, the army and police get a grip on the situation, but by then it is too late; any valuables have already disappeared. The Royal Thai Army does a fine job clearing roads to aid the search for and recovery of bodies, which are taken in trucks to nearby Buddhist temples.

The temples are unable to cope with the constantly rising number of corpses. Volunteers try to slow the decomposition process, using dry ice. The victims are photographed and tagged with identification codes. This should help relatives to identify lost loved ones, but in fact it just causes more confusion – because the faces of most victims have become unidentifiable, being black and swollen.

After Day Two it is not even possible to distinguish between Thai and foreigner. DNA samples are taken from relatives to support the identification process.

Two stranded dolphins and a dugong are rescued alive from the Khao Lak reservoir. They are inoculated and returned to the sea. Even this one ray of hope cannot disguise the fact that Thailand faces its saddest New Year in history.

January 2005: Clean-up efforts begin in earnest. Bang Niang beach is the first to be cleared. Severely damaged structures are demolished, and bodies are still being found. The search teams decide to pump all the water from the reservoir at Bang Niang. More than 250 bodies have been recovered from there so far, including the drowned passengers from a large tour bus.

International Disaster Victim Identification (DVI) teams arrive in Thai Muang to aid in the process of identifying bodies.

Many people hope that their relatives will be identified quickly now, but with a death toll of 5,000 and rising, the chances of speedy closure are reduced.

A bizarre kind of tsunami tourism starts, which does Khao Lak no good at all. Buses from Phuket arrive, and I get reports about a Japanese tourist posing beside a truck full of victims’ bodies for a “Kodak moment”. He is seen making the Victory sign. Luckily I did not see this, as I would have knocked him out on the spot.

February 2005: Huge trucks bring in load after load of soil while heavy machinery works to replace lost stretches of shoreline and remodel the washed-away beaches.

Some of the larger resorts are granted reconstruction loans by banks, only to be told that the money will be made available “later this year”. Small businesses and small family resorts are left to their own devices.

Small and medium enterprises in Bang Niang unite, the goal being to rebuild as many bungalows as possible in the shortest possible time to help revive the area. Despite the massive loss of life, the spirit of the surviving business people and residents remains unbroken.

With just a few exceptions, most residents have returned to build a new future, and that also means a new Khao Lak. An unbelievable 26 resorts are operational again, five of them right on the beaches.

March 2005: In the middle of the night of March 28, an earthquake registering 8.7 on the Richter Scale rocks northern Sumatra. There is panic, which is quite understandable. The combined and immediate efforts of police, army and volunteers to evacuate potential danger areas of Khao Lak prove successful, with hundreds of people arriving in safe zones just minutes after the first warning is issued. Luckily, the feared tsunami does not happen.

On February 19, a definite “go” for reconstruction is issued by the government. Countless foreign volunteers are helping to rebuild resorts, homes and shelters all around Khao Lak – while at the same time being threatened with dire penalties by the Employment Services Office should they fail to obtain work permits. In fact, nothing happens.

Most local tsunami victims will have to rebuild their lives and businesses using the maximum financial government help of 20,000 baht per person. Rain starts to fall in March, and everyone hopes that vegetation will soon cover the scarred dry red soil in the affected areas. In mid-March, the Bang Niang beach area is still without power.

April to November 2005: Many survivors, relatives and friends of tsunami victims find a sense of relief and closure during the Khao Lak 100 days Tsunami Memorial Festival, a three-day event from April 2-4 that transforms the community.

The Ministry of Public Health opens the Mental Health Recovery Center in Khao Lak on May 26 to help tsunami victims recovering from Post Traumatic Stress Disorder and depression. It is long overdue, but better late than never.

Reconstruction efforts continue everywhere and, in late June, most of the promised bank loans are paid and the bigger players really start to rock the boat. It is now becoming obvious that Khao Lak is not only on the way back, it will perhaps emerge from this ordeal stronger than ever.

The message is clear, and it floods in from lovers of Khao Lak all over the world, in emails, on Internet forums and in phone calls: “We will come back!”

Krabi bounces back
By GUS REYNOLDS

Krabi was in a unique position immediately after the tsunami as the mainland escaped the heavy damage, destruction and loss of life that the province’s islands sustained. As a result, the provincial center of Krabi Town was able to react quickly to provide aid to those areas that had experienced the greatest devastation.

The best-known island group, and the one most exposed to the tsunami, was Phi Phi: the densely populated section of Ton Sai Bay on Phi Phi Don sustained massive damage and suffered the highest proportion of fatalities and casualties in the province.

The Phi Phi Islands fall under the jurisdiction of the Ao Nang Tambon Administration Organization (OrBorTor), and Ao Nang Beach became the focal point for rescue and evacuation operations for the people of Phi Phi and surrounding islands.

Ao Nang Beach itself was in relatively good condition, mainly because it was protected from the waves by many of the outer islands. Its sea wall also minimized the amount of damage to beach-front properties.

Ao Nang beachfront was therefore suitable as the staging point for operations, with the beach road closed to facilitate the movement of emergency vehicles.

Since the mainland infrastructure was relatively unscathed, the resources of the Krabi Municipality reacted in full force, aiding the victims from the other islands and areas affected by the tsunami. Krabi Hospital was soon packed to capacity, however, and the Chinese temple in Krabi Town was quickly converted into a temporary morgue.

Krabi Town was one of the quickest to respond to the reconstruction and relaunching of long-tail boats. Haad Nopporat Thara Beach was set up as a building site, using the concrete blocks and broken construction materials from the unfinished seawall as building platforms.

Volunteer students and scuba divers also helped the operators of a large crane that pulled hundreds of boats out of the bay.

With government aid, funds from large international foundations and private donations, the mayor put together a team to co-ordinate these activities. The first aim was to identify properly registered boats, and to try to discourage any fraudulent claims.

Once the boat owners had been verified, construction needs were recorded, materials purchased and even volunteer mechanics arranged to repair engines.

In Krabi Town, foreign volunteers living in the province rushed to Krabi Hospital and the morgue, providing translation services for families trying to locate their loved ones.

Almost all of the Thai residents of Phi Phi were relocated into two areas of Krabi Town, Klong Hin and Nong Kok. Various relief agencies provided food and support, helping the survivors move into their new homes.

Help International Phi Phi – HiPhiPhi – under the leadership of Dutch national Emiel Kok, was instrumental in cataloging and identifying the children who had lost family members and parents.

This group provided much-needed resources, and acted as a bridge between the activities on Phi Phi Island and the relocation centers in Krabi Town.

HiPhiPhi later handed responsibility for sponsorship of the children to the Sriphong Phuk-aoluan Foundation (www. krabirelief.com). This foundation has taken on the task of monitoring and supporting child victims of the tsunami until they reach the age of 18.

As Chuan Phukaoluan, President of the Foundation, states, “Both the public and private sectors did some great work after the tsunami, but now it is [the foundation’s] job to deal with the long-term effects of the tsunami, relating to unemployment and job creation, education of the children and continued medical care for the victims.”

Restabilizing the community torn apart by the catastrophe has been a priority. Relocation of the children to different school districts solved one problem – the over-burdening of one school with too many new students – but at the same time it also separated children from their friends, families and support systems.

Unemployment skyrocketed as the tourism industry slumped, and most tourist-related businesses reduced staff levels by as much as half. In response, several different groups and foundations became active in reuniting the communities and providing support for occupational training.

The Aree Foundation, as well as Tex’s Rock Climbing, have worked hard to provide assets to help establish a safety net. For instance, while the initial rebuilding of the long-tail boats was considered a success, it soon became apparent that because of the slowdown in tourist arrivals, the boatmen were still facing economic ruin. Private donors, along with the local foundations, helped to refit many of these boats as fishing boats to enable the owners to earn some income.

The revival of tourism will help solve some of the problems faced by the tsunami-affected families and children.

Key to many tourists is the feeling of safety, that there is no chance of being surprised by a natural disaster as many were on that morning of last December 26.

The government understands this issue, and has been setting up a tsunami-warning system in Krabi Province. In total there are 35 sirens throughout the province, including at Koh Lanta, Ao Luek, Koh Phi Phi and Nuea Klong.

In September, the Governor of Krabi oversaw the implementation of the early-warning alarm system in Ao Nang. Evacuation routes were established and meeting areas designated. A drill on September 23 saw a large turn-out of residents and rescue personnel.

A visit to most areas of Krabi Province finds the infrastructure intact, businesses open and the hotels 100% ready to accept tourists.

The rebound in tourism will play a vital role in reducing unemployment and making many of the affected families self-sufficient again.

In the meantime, many organizations are providing assistance to aid those children and others still needing help until the community can be restabilized.

A story of survival and triumph over tragedy

‘If there’s something you’ve not said to a loved one, make sure you do, because you don’t want to be left with regret. Your whole life can change in one second.’


SHIONA MACKENZIE talks to Claire Lester, a teacher at BCIS, who lost two siblings and her father in the disaster

“I opened my eyes, but couldn’t see a thing in the thick, black churning water. My body was being pummeled by what felt like blocks of cement. And then I thought, ‘this is what it’s like to drown’.”

When the tsunami struck Koh Phi Phi Don, Blackpool-born Claire Lester was enjoying Day 3 of her family’s first Christmas holiday together outside England. The Lesters had rented a row of five bungalows at the PP Princess Diving & Spa Resort, a few meters from the beach.

“We’d always spent Christmas in England, but with me living on Phuket, it was a chance for us to share a special holiday in tropical paradise,” says Claire, an art teacher at the British Curriculum International School (BCIS).

Her father, Keith, her older brother, Adrian, and her older sister, Amanda, all died in the tsunami. Claire survived, along with her mother, Barbara, her younger brother James, his partner Lucy Haylock and Claire’s partner, Surin “Rin” Homhoun.

“The couple of days we had on Phi Phi as a family were wonderful. We were getting along famously and enjoying being together… I miss my father, bro-ther and sister so much. The pain never goes away, but you can do things to make it easier to bear. Although I still work hard, I ‘play’ harder than before. I am thankful to be alive.

“Survivors of the tsunami know what it’s like to be drowning – to hear and feel the indescribable noise of the sea,” Claire says. “After going through that, I’ve become aware of my own mortality. The experience brought home to me the importance of family and the brevity of life.

“If there’s something you’ve not said to a loved one, make sure you do, because you don’t want to be left with regret. Your whole life can change in one second.”

While Lucy was taken to Mission Hospital in Phuket, most of Koh Phi Phi Don’s victims were sent to Krabi within 48 hours.

Claire commends the quality of care at Krabi Hospital. “The staff was really well organized. We were cleaned, given wristbands indicating the extent of our injuries and taken to different areas for treatment.”

With the sudden influx of patients requiring procedures, there weren’t enough operating rooms available, but Claire’s four severed tendons in her right foot needed urgent surgery. It was performed in the emergency room under local anesthesia.

“I was told there weren’t enough resources to cope. It was okay with me; I still didn’t feel any pain in my leg or foot. The operation was over quickly and my leg was put in a cast.”

On December 30, Claire, Barbara, James and Lucy were taken to Bumrungrad Hospital, Bangkok, by helicopter for further treatment. Their lacerations – first immersed in dirty water, then exposed for several hours – had become infected. Claire’s leg was swollen and required two more operations. K. Rin followed on.

In Bangkok, the British Ambassador and his wife visited regularly, bringing scones and other favorites to lift their spirits. They discussed the effectiveness of the British Embassy’s emergency response.

The Lesters returned to Phuket in February. That month and the next, Claire and James received daily physiotherapy sessions at Bangkok-Phuket Hospital. Barbara, James, K. Rin and Claire also received trauma and grief counseling.

Then, they flew to England with the bodies of Claire’s older siblings.

On an April day in Black-pool, the Lesters held a memorial service which celebrated the lives of Claire’s older siblings and her father, who was still missing.

“My physical wounds had healed sufficiently for me to confront the emotional aspect,” she says. “I had to ask myself if I wanted to continue living on Phuket.”

She embarked upon what would be a slow but steady journey to full recovery. Claire decided to go back to work. “That helped me make up my mind to stay in Phuket. It is my home now.”

Claire’s mother, James and Lucy traveled with her to Phuket once again, staying with her for two weeks in May.

Then, just two days after Barbara, James and Lucy had flown back to England, Keith’s body was identified. Claire accompanied her father’s body back to England.

The Lesters played a key role in a memorial service for tsunami victims attended by the Cabinet and the Queen in Lon-don’s St Paul’s Cathedral. “My mother and I led one part of the ceremony called ‘act of remembrance’, leading the procession of representative families down the center aisle to lay flowers on the altar,” she says.

They were also invited to meet several VIPs, including British Prime Minister Tony Blair, Foreign Secretary Jack Straw and Prince Charles, who wanted to hear what they had to say about improving disaster recovery operations.

“Prince Charles was genuinely interested in the Clean Up the World Day event I helped organize at Kamala Beach in September,” says Claire.

This is Claire’s fourth year in her job at BCIS as an art teacher and Creativity Action and Service group (CAS) Co-ordinator. Clean Up the World Day 2005, organized by 13 International Baccalaureate students as part of the CAS program, was very meaningful for Claire. It was the first time, since the tsunami, she was on a beach for longer than 30 minutes. She carried on, despite a heavy storm that raged throughout the day.

“I can sit on a beach and feel at ease again,” she says. “It helped me conquer my fear; I have been on a speedboat and swum in the sea – things I thought I could never bring myself to do again. The more that I face my fears, the easier it becomes.”

Just working and maintaining a daily routine has proved beneficial, says Claire, who returned to work again full time in August – eight months after the tsunami.

Her family and friends began to help tsunami victims by initiating fund-raising drives in England. Their efforts garnered some 214,000 baht (3,000 pounds sterling) – presented to Krabi Hospital in May, to buy a badly needed portable ultrasound scanner.

For the PP Princess and PP Charlie Tsunami Relief Fund, they raised more than 185,000 baht. The PP Princess Diving & Spa Resort lost more than 100 foreign guests to the tsunami and some 30 staff members who left behind more than 20 orphans.

The Lesters also helped two Blackpool area schools raise money for Phuket’s Life Home Project, the shelter for mothers and their children who are HIV positive or have AIDS.

Claire continues to receive support from regular counseling at Bangkok-Phuket Hospital and a counseling group at Surin Beach Resort – Tsunami Victims Aid Initiative.

“Flashbacks don’t just disappear,” she says. “There are people who, although not physically affected by the tsunami, were affected mentally and the anniversary will bring this trauma out. For me, it serves as a constant reminder to enjoy life to the fullest. Living here, I have been able to face my fears straight away. I think it has been harder for my mother and my brother in England to come to terms with everything.”

Warned that the injuries she had sustained might prevent her from walking normally again, she took it as a challenge to refortify her mind and body.

“They told me in hospital, ‘You’re not going to be able to do anything for six months; you probably aren’t going to be able to walk properly.’ That was a big issue for me,” Claire says, “but now I do yoga, tap dancing and Thai kick-boxing.

“It feels good to get back into tap dancing, which I started when I was about four. It really gets my circulation going. The exercise, plus physiotherapy, has gone a long way to healing my leg and improving my mobility.”

Since the tsunami, Claire’s interest in holistic medicine has grown. “Before the new school year, I did a 10-day fast on Koh Samui. I started cranial sacral therapy, too, as I had been feeling tired and emotional with the build-up to the anniversary. Focused on healing, I also had reiki and acupuncture for my leg.

“I believe in using anything you can find to make the day easier, or to get through something. It doesn’t matter what it is. For me, it’s these.”

The Economic Impact


In addition to the loss of life, economic damage to the whole region was huge.

RICHARD WATSON looks at the economic impact of the disaster, and predicts a rosy future for Phuket

The full impact of the tsunami on the economy of Phuket is not easy to assess. But what is plain is that, because the disaster happened early in the tourist season, it severely limited the income that would normally have been earned during this period. In addition, much of the structural damage sustained was, in many cases, uninsured.

The tourist season is the main income period for many business owners and individuals on the island; to have an event such as this happen so early in the season increased the financial damage considerably.

It can be assumed that the financial damage from the tsunami was mostly felt by hotels, bars, dive companies, car hire companies, and other tourism-related businesses. In reality, the damage was more widespread.

A simple example of this might be a car salesman working for a dealership in Phuket City. He is not directly effected by the tsunami, but the company he works for finds that sales have slumped and he is made redundant. He may have been on the verge of having a house built and was also going to buy furniture. So the loss extends to the builder and furniture shops.

The knock-on effect was felt through a substantial part of Phuket’s economy. Turnover in, say, Central Festival shopping center was severely reduced because local people found themselves in straitened circumstances, in addition to the absence of the normal number of tourists.

Phuket, as a tourist destination, is to a great extent dependent on image. Most tourists seek sun and sand, a getaway from the monotony of everyday living and commuting in chilly, gray cities. In particular, Phuket’s tourist season coincides with the northern hemisphere’s most unpleasant weather.

However, the image of a tropical paradise is shattered when scenes of devastation and massive loss of life suddenly appear on potential tourists’ TV screens. It jars with what the average tourist is seeking in a holiday destination.

Often, anecdotal information is interesting in such situations. A friend who lost his diving job because of the tsunami took a six-month holiday to New Zealand. In Auckland he went to a travel agency in July to reconfirm airline tickets to Phuket, only to hear the travel agent advise a local couple that a holiday to Phuket was out of the question. “The place is devastated,” he heard.

Fortunately, as every politician in the world is aware, public memory is short and many people are better informed than the travel agent in New Zealand.

A married couple I know tried to get a room at Amanpuri in September, only to be told that it was fully booked. Determined to visit Phuket for the first time, they ended up renting an Aman-puri villa, complete with five bedrooms and two swimming pools, just for themselves!

What does the future hold for Phuket? It promises to be exceptional; the fact is that there is a limited number of tropical island destinations in the world.

Combine this with the fact that now there are more affluent people in the world than ever before, plus the increasing ease of travel and ever-improving amenities and communications and the result is that more and more people are traveling.

Phuket also caters to a wide variety of tourists, not only in terms of many nationalities but also in terms of different income groups.

In addition to “normal” tourism, there are those who are seeking vacation and retirement homes. The number of new developments under construction on this island reflects just how large the market is.

In addition, new marinas are in evidence all over; this is a very welcome addition, as boat owners have to spend money. The old definition of a yacht is “a hole in the sea that you fill with money”; a new definition of “boat” is “bring out another thousand”.

I am told by those in the industry that hotel reservations are excellent for the rest of the season.

One minor drag on the budget tourist sector may be that the increase in fuel prices adds to airfares. On the other hand, the Japanese economy is showing strong signs of recovery and, for the first time in 15 years, this is not an export-led blip; domestic Japanese spending is now on track – and that means tourism.

For the tourist who wishes to have a safe vacation in Phuket, the real risk is not another tsunami but renting a motorcycle. I take my boat to sea without a second thought, but wild horses could not drag me onto a motorcycle.
