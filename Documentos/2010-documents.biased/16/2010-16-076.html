Inside Jobs: What's ailing the Apple chief?

Stephen Hutcheon in San Francisco
January 6, 2009 - 1:14PM

The most famous pancreas in America is the one belonging to Apple CEO Steve Jobs.

Even though the "p" word wasn't mentioned in a statement he released overnight, Jobs's memo about his weight loss condition has set off a chain reaction of interest in all things pancreatic.

The 53-year-old Apple co-founder underwent surgery in 2004 to remove a malignant tumour in his pancreas, an organ responsible for producing hormones and enzymes that are vital to the digestive and metabolic system.

In his "letter" addressed to the Apple community, Jobs attempted to quell speculation about his health by ascribing his physical condition to a "hormone imbalance" that is "robbing me of the proteins my body needs to be healthy".

Without going into more detail, Jobs said the remedy for what he described as a "nutritional problem" was "relatively simple and straightforward" and that he expected the imbalance to be fixed within the next few months.

Jobs is a notoriously private man despite his leading role in both the technology and entertainment worlds, the latter via his board seat at Disney where he is the largest individual shareholder.

He ended his letter with this testy sign off: "So now I've said more than I wanted to say, and all that I am going to say, about this."

While the letter has killed off the Steve-on-his-deathbed rumours, it hasn't stopped journalists and bloggers from trying to find out exactly what it is that Jobs is suffering from.

The Bloomberg news agency spoke to Dr Simon Lo, director of the pancreas diseases program at the Cedars-Sinai Medical Centre in Los Angeles, who suggested that Jobs might be suffering from a digestive condition known as dumping syndrome.

Surgeons who operated on Jobs in 2004 might have used the so-called Whipple procedure, which involves removing parts of the stomach, pancreas, bile duct and small intestine.

Lo said that, if it was a case of dumping syndrome, patients would be advised to alter their diet - including avoiding fats and carbohydrates - and eat more frequent and smaller servings.

However, Dr Harold Frucht, an associate professor at Columbia University, told ABC News that it was more likely that Jobs's surgeon performed an enucleation - a procedure in which the tumour is scooped out of the pancreas, leaving the organ largely intact.

Dr Michael Jensen, an endocrinologist at the Mayo Clinic, told The Wall Street Journal's health blog that there were two possibilities.

The first and more serious was that the tumour might have recurred, upsetting the Apple chief's hormonal system. In this case surgery would be required.

The second scenario is that, as a result of the tumour surgery in 2004, Jobs's pancreas was now not large enough to produce the enzymes and hormones necessary to have an efficient digestive system.

In this case, taking supplemental enzyme pills would do the trick, Jensen said.

Speaking to MSNBC.com, Dr Jeffrey Mechanick, an endocrinologist with the Mount Sinai Hospital in New York, concurred with Jensen's view that the condition could mean a recurrence of Jobs's islet cell neuroendocrine tumour - a rare but treatable cancer.

Islet cell neuroendocrine tumours account for only about 3 per cent of pancreatic cancer cases in the US. Luckily for Jobs, this less common variety is also less deadly, especially if detected early and removed.

Actor Patrick Swayze is also suffering from pancreatic cancer. Unfortunately for him, he has the more common and hence more deadly variety.

Stephen Hutcheon travelled to San Francisco as a guest of Apple.
